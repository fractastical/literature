## Key Claims and Hypotheses

1. **The Central Argument:** The paper posits that genuine scientific discovery requires a shift in AI architecture, moving beyond current limitations to integrate active inference principles, persistent scientific memory, and robust causal reasoning.

2. **The Abstraction Gap Hypothesis:** A fundamental barrier to AI-driven discovery is the disconnect between low-level statistical regularities and the mechanistic concepts that scientists actually use to reason.

3. **The Reasoning Gap Hypothesis:** Current AI models are limited to correlation-driven pattern completion rather than causal, counterfactual inference – a crucial distinction for scientific understanding.

4. **The Reality Gap Hypothesis:** The disconnect between computation and the empirical feedback loops that ultimately arbitrate truth represents a significant obstacle.

5. **The Proposed Architecture:** The authors advocate for a modular active inference architecture incorporating: (a) an abstraction layer for translating between different levels of representation; (b) persistent scientific memory to maintain conceptual knowledge; and (c) verification pathways to ground speculation in empirical reality.

6. **The Importance of Human-AI Collaboration:** The paper highlights the indispensable role of human judgment in guiding exploration, recognizing meaningful patterns, and making value judgments.

7. **The Need for Multi-Modal Representations:** The authors argue that AI systems must be able to integrate diverse data modalities (e.g., continuous fields, discrete events, symbolic constructs) to capture the complexity of scientific phenomena.
