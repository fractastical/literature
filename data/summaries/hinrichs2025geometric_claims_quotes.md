## Key Claims and Hypotheses

1.  **Core Claim:** The paper proposes a novel framework for understanding social cognition – second-person active inference – that models social interaction as a recursive inference process where agents dynamically track and regulate their own generative models in relation to others.

2.  **Hypothesis:** Affect emerges as a consequence of recursive inference over self-model coherence, weighted by self-relevance and modulated by prior affective states and temporal aiming.

3.  **Core Claim:** The authors introduce “geometric hyperscanning,” a method to operationalize affect by tracking topological reconfiguration in inter-brain networks, using Forman-Ricci curvature (FRc) as a proxy for affective phase transitions (rupture, co-regulation, re-attunement).

4.  **Hypothesis:** The recursive coupling of agents’ generative models, as measured by FRc, can be used to predict and understand the dynamics of social interaction, particularly in terms of affective regulation.

5.  **Core Claim:** The paper argues that traditional third-person approaches to social cognition (e.g., mentalizing) fail to capture the dynamic, embodied nature of social interaction, which is better understood through a second-person perspective.
