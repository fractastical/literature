## Key Claims and Hypotheses

1.  The paper proposes a framework for modelling cognitive states using Hilbert space techniques, arguing that this approach offers a consistent alternative to classical Bayesian updating.
2.  The core hypothesis is that the dynamics of a person’s mind, when considering choices, can be characterised by the von Neumann-Lu¨ders projection postulate, which inherently minimizes future ‘surprises’.
3.  The paper posits that the key difference between classical and quantum approaches to cognitive modelling lies in the representation of the state of mind as a vector in a high-dimensional Hilbert space, which naturally accounts for entanglement and superposition.
4.  The authors argue that the projection postulate provides a geometrically intuitive explanation for phenomena such as confirmation bias, where individuals tend to cling to initial beliefs despite contradictory evidence.
5.  The paper suggests that the compatibility of choices is a key factor in cognitive behaviour, and that incompatibility can lead to a failure to escape from false attractors.
6.  The authors propose that the dynamics of the state of mind can be described by a stochastic differential equation, which is consistent with the free energy principle.
