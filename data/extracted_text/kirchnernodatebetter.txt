1. Depression: a social disorder 18 
Since Coyne's (1976)  influential characterization of depression as an interactional 19 
disorder, there h as been a large body of research on the social factors involved in the 20 
development and maintenance of depressive symptoms.  By now, there is broad consensus that 21 
individuals with depression show marked distortions in the anticipation, perception, and 22 
processing of social information, as well as in their social behavior. For example, depressed 23 
individuals are more sensitive to rejection (Gao et al., 2017)  and perform considerably worse 24 
at tasks measuring theory of mind (Nestor et al., 2022); they also reveal poor social problem -25 
solving when interacting with others (e.g., Hames et al., 2013). 26 
While social impairments are well described in depression (for reviews, see Bird et al., 27 
2018; Hames et al., 2013; Kupferberg et al., 2016; Segrin, 1990, 2000; Starr & Davila, 2008; 28 
Tse & Bond, 2004; Weightman et al., 2014) , a computational account is lacking of how these 29 
impairments differ from healthy social functioning and why they develop, are maintained, and 30 
manifest in such different ways (Smith, Badcock, et al., 2021) . Because of its high degree of 31 
formalization and flexibility, a computational model of these processes would enable 32 
researchers to functionally link heterogeneous social symptoms in depression to various biases 33 
in patients‚Äô internal social belief systems, which would have important implications for 34 
diagnostics, etiology, and tailored interventions (Kirchner, Eckert, et al., 2022; Schwartenbeck 35 
et al., 2015; Smith, Badcock, et al., 2021).1 In this paper, we describe the basic architecture of 36 
the model we have in mind ‚Äì and focus on its motivation and functional form (as opposed to 37 
presenting numerical analyses with a particular example).  Possible application and 38 
operationalization ideas will be described in the "Implications and Future Research" section. 39

2.  On the maintenance of social symptoms in depression 63 
One important question is why social perception and behavior do not normalize when 64 
the social context improves. Explanations from general and clinical psychology suggest several 65 
mechanisms are involved in this phenomenon - for example, learned helplessness (Seligman, 66 
1972), neurobiological dysfunction (Porcelli et al., 2019), greater neural effort processing social 67 
cues (Suffel et al., 2020), pre-operational thinking (J. P. McCullough, 2003), dysfunctional core 68 
beliefs (Beck, 1963, 1964), negative relational schemas (Baldwin, 1992), increased elaboration 69 
of negative information (Kasch et al., 2002), blunted elaboration of positive information (Chen 70 
et al., 2015), persistent negative expectations (Kube et al., 2020; Roepke & Seligman, 2016) , 71 
and cognitive immunization (Rief & Joormann, 2019). 72 
Although we believe all these factors play an important role, we suggest that it is 73 
primarily how (and by what model  of the social world) individuals with depression infer the 74 
characteristics of social contexts through social  action (e.g., social decision making) that 75 
contributes to the maintenance of these symptoms (compare with ‚Äúassimilation‚Äù, Panitz et al., 76 
2021). Thus, clinical observations indicate that individuals with depressive symptoms (and 77 
specifically those with a social -trauma history) reveal distortions in exploring their 78 
interpersonal context before making social decisions (e.g., choosing to withdraw from a social 79 
situation). Instead, they stick to schema -congruent social strategies (e.g., "better -safe-than-80 
sorry", Badcock et al., 2017) that prevent new learning and promote ‚Äúfitting‚Äù schema-congruent 81 
experiences ("As I said, no one talked to me at the party"). This notion is supported by research 82 
linking depression to social withdrawal (e.g., Seidel et al., 2010) , highly inhibited behavior 83 
(e.g., Kasch et al., 2002), low social information seeking, passivity and social avoidance (e.g., 84 
D‚ÄôIuso et al., 2018) , as well as higher levels of distrust, shame, and disappointment in social 85 
situations (e.g., Fern√°ndez-Theoduloz et al., 2019). There is also evidence that depressed people 86

3.1 Active inference  133 
In the past decades, emerging computational theories have changed the way/how we 134 
think about the mind in health and disease. In cognitive and computational neuroscience , the 135

active inference framework enables us to unify complex processes like perception, learning, 136 
action, and decision making under a Bayesian decision-theoretic umbrella (for a synthesis, see 137 
Da Costa et al., 2020; for decision -theoretic underpinnings, see Huys et al., 2015) . Central to 138 
active inference is the notion of the mind as an elaborate inference engine, trying to make sense 139 
of its environment from noisy, ambiguous, and partial sensory information (Friston et al., 2017).  140 
The ‚Äúreality‚Äù of the external world is not directly accessible to the agent ‚Äì the state of these 141 
hidden factors needs to be inferred with the help of an agent‚Äôs internal, generative model (Heins 142 
et al., 2022; Smith et al., 2022b). The generative model contains the agent‚Äôs beliefs about how 143 
certain sensory information relates to an environmental factor.  In other words, how latent 144 
factors or states cause observable (sensory) consequences. This probabilistic model of the world 145 
must be continuously updated (i.e., ‚Äúoptimized‚Äù) by sensory input to ensure the correct 146 
inference of hidden states and for maintaining physiological ‚Äì and psychosocial - homeostasis. 147 
It is matched with an agent‚Äôs prior beliefs about certain hidden states or observations (Friston 148 
et al., 2016; Smith, Badcock, et al., 2021) . Accurate priors and generative models are crucial 149 
for the agent‚Äôs survival and for maintaining physiological homeostasis (for further information, 150 
see Da Costa et al., 2020).  151 
A final component of active inference is action. By performing actions or action 152 
sequences (i.e., policies), the agent exerts an influence on hidden factors (Heins et al., 2022) . 153 
Inferring policies allows the agent to select action sequences that lead to desired or preferred 154 
sensory information, that is, actions minimize surprise in the long term. In the context of active 155 
inference, we can therefore speak of ‚ÄúPredictions, not Com mands‚Äù (Adams et al., 2013) : the 156 
motor system executes actions that lead to the  expected sensory information. This basic view 157 
of sentient behavior can be summarized as choosing courses of action or plans that bring about 158 
predicted consequences. This basic idea operates at the level of simple reflexes, through to 159 
social narratives. In  terms of simple movements, our actions can be viewed as fulfilling top -160 
down predictions of a proprioceptive sort that supply setpoints or equilibrium points (Feldman 161

& Levin, 1995). This is very reminiscent of ideomotor theory in the 19th-century (Wiese, 2017). 162 
When applied to social behaviors and decision -making, this is often cast as planning as 163 
inference (Attias, 2003; Botvinick & Toussaint, 2012; Kaplan & Friston, 2018); in other words, 164 
committing to those courses of action that minimize the deviation from predicted or expected 165 
outcomes. 166 
Mathematically speaking, we can approximate the minimizing of ‚Äúsurprise‚Äù by 167 
minimizing a quantity named ‚Äúfree energy‚Äù (FE). In the context of active inference, minimizing 168 
FE should lead to ‚Äúoptimal‚Äù perceptions and actions (for further information, see Friston, 2010). 169 
More generally, actions or policies are selected that minimize  expected free energy (Da Costa 170 
et al., 2021). Technically, expected free energy subsumes two kinds of (Bayes) optimality. The 171 
first reflects the propensity to bring about preferred outcomes (e.g., being at home), which are 172 
just those ‚Äòstates of being‚Äô that characterize the decision maker or person in question. 173 
Mathematically, this preference -seeking can be read as maximizing expected value, where 174 
value is just the (log) probability of being in a characteristic state. The other part of expected 175 
free energy underwrites information -seeking and the resolution of the uncertaint y inherent in 176 
minimizing expected surprise. Mathematically, this is usually expressed as expected 177 
information gain. In short, active inference entails a joint commitment to optimal Bayesian 178 
decisions (via maximizing expected value) (Berger, 2011) while, at the same time, complying 179 
with the principles of optimum Bayesian design (via maximizing expected information gain) 180 
(Lindley, 1956; MacKay, 1992) . In effect, this accommodates the exploration -exploitation 181 
dilemma by placing epistemic and pragmatic imperatives on the same footing (Schwartenbeck 182 
et al., 2019). 183 
As a simplified example, consider a man who needs to infer his physical location in a 184 
town. He has no direct access to this information (hidden factor), but needs to infer it from the 185 
visual, auditory, and olfactory information his sensory organs take in from his environment. 186

The agent‚Äôs observation model contains his specific beliefs about how certain sensory 187 
information relates to a certain neighborhood or location in space (e.g., a sign to a specific area). 188 
The agent implicitly forms ‚Äúhypotheses‚Äù about where he is located given his latest sensory 189 
information (the likelihood). Given such sensory information, the hypothesis with the highest 190 
posterior probability wins. Furthermore, his previous actions (i.e. walking down an avenue, 191 
then turning right, etc.) provide information about  his location in space. Future actions or 192 
policies are selected so that the agent‚Äôs desired sensory information is acquired. If the agent 193 
wants to go home, only those policies will be chosen that will lead to the expected visual, 194 
auditory, and olfactory information usually experienced while living at home.  195 
From an evolutionary psychology perspective, active inference constantly involves 196 
making a trade-off between seeking new information through action (in this example, exploring 197 
the city) and maximizing access to known rewards (returning home quickly) in order to survive 198 
in an ever-changing environment (explore vs. exploit dilemma, Gopnik, 2020; Kouvaris et al., 199 
2015). Agents failing to engage in any exploratory behavior who only seek to maximize known 200 
rewards ‚Äúover -fit‚Äù their environmen t, and run the risk of being incapable of adapting to 201 
environmental changes (i.e., not finding home when the cityscape changes). Conversely, 202 
‚Äúunder-fitting‚Äù agents who engage in a lot of explo 203 
atory behavior and show little reward maximization run the risk  of failing to exploit 204 
resources and rewards in order to maintain their fitness (i.e., never find home). 205 
In mental health terms, active inference suggests that mental disorders, like depression 206 
are the result of biased inference of hidden states of the wor ld (although it is probably not the 207 
inference algorithm itself that is biased, but rather the generative model to which it is applied; 208 
Schwartenbeck et al., 2015) . In this context, Bayesian formulations have proved useful in 209 
accounting for diff erent symptom domains of MDD (Chekroud, 2015; Consta nt et al., 2019; 210

Smith et al., 2018). Smith and colleagues (2018), for example, suggest that depressive schemas3 211 
may act as precise, internalized priors in patients with depression. Given a certain set of sensory 212 
information, the interpretation, or ‚Äúhypothesis‚Äù, with the highest posterior probability is 213 
selected to explain the sensory information.4 In depression, this may be the depressive schema. 214 
Moreover, they suggest that the core symptom of psychomotor slowing and loss of energy may 215 
result from biased beliefs regarding an action‚Äôs utility in achieving a desired  sensory state 216

5. Implications and future research 307 
Our approach enables a new  formalization of the active inference of social context 308 
characteristics in mentally healthy and ill individuals, which allows researchers to functionally 309 
link heterogeneous social phenomena in depression to different biases in patient‚Äôs internal 310 
social belief systems. In consequence, it offers new perspectives for both etiological research 311 
in depression as well as improved diagnostics and treatment. Knowing how social trauma or the 312 
lack of social support affects certain parameters of the generative model and which parameters 313 
(or parameter configurations) are predestined for certain social experiences and behaviors has 314 
profound implications for prevention and treatment -selection, since treatment strategies affect 315 
the patient‚Äôs generative model‚Äôs parameters differently. To stay with the above -mentioned 316 
example: Individuals with "too low" and barely deviant scores on parameter p(s1) (i.e., with 317 
great uncertainty about which social context they will enter), are likely to benefit most from 318 
interventions that reduce social safety behaviors and provide consistent reinforcement schedule 319 
within the therapeutic relationship, since these interventions foster beliefs in the predictability 320 
of social contexts in the long term. Conversely, individuals with a strong negative  prior (i.e., 321 
who are deeply convinced they will enter a negative social context in the future) are more likely 322 
to benefit from reparenting techniques and interpersonal discrimination training, as they 323 
AN ACTIVE INFERENCE ACCOUNT TO SOCIAL INFERENCE IN DEPRESSION 
improve social context discrimination. This example il lustrates that different parameter 324 
specifications in the generative model can lead to various treatment implications. Moreover, it 325 
illustrates how the treatment of depressive disorders can be individualized by our 326 
conceptualization, even when different distortions in the same parameter are involved.  327 
In a similar way, the approach we suggest could deepen our understanding of the neural 328 
implementation of related processes. While the literature has yielded quite heterogeneous 329 
findings on the neural correlates  of depression (Gray et al., 2020)  a better description of 330 
impairments on a computational level might reduce variability across studies and reveal more 331 
suitable approaches for tailored interventions (e.g., brain stimulation, Brunoni et al., 2017). 332 
Finally, our conceptualization of social biases in depression sheds light on the issue of 333 
treatment-resistant depression (Schramm et al., 2020). It seems plausible that the interventions 334 
these patients undergo often fail to address the ‚Äúright‚Äù parameters in the respective generative 335 
models. For example, no matter how many positive social experiences a depressed patient may 336 
have with other people during ther apy, if he or she believes that positive social cues are 337 
unrelated to the social context‚Äôs actual nature (concerns the observational model p(oT|sT)), then 338 
these experiences have no impact on the generative model.  339 
From an etiological and developmental pers pective, research should examine further 340 
which social experiences affect model parameters over the life span. This would also constitute 341 
a thorough test of complex interpersonal theories on the development of depressive disorders, 342 
such as those assumed in attachment theory, schema therapy or the Cognitive -Behavioral 343 
Analysis System of Psychotherapy (CBASP). In this context, we assume that our model can 344 
also be applied to interpersonal experience and behavior in other mental disorders like 345 
personality disorders or social phobia (e.g., Mikulincer & Shaver, 2012).  346 
Furthermore, research should focus on accurately diag nosing patient‚Äôs generative 347 
models, linking them to concrete treatment recommendations. From the practitioner's 348

perspective, the relevant questions are (a) how to diagnose generative models in practice and 349 
(b) which interventions are most suitable to influ ence certain model parameters. These 350 
recommendations could be followed in turn to better tailor the treatment of depressive disorders. 351 
In this context, we suggest adapting experimental paradigms from social psychology (e.g., 352 
Wirth, 2016)  or game theory (e.g., Wang et al., 2015) , as these paradigms require social 353 
decisions based on inferences about social contexts and can be easily applied in practice as well 354 
as formalized in terms of the model above (cf. Smith et al., 2022). For example, social decision 355 
making in cooperative games that require inferences about social contexts (e.g., evaluating a 356 
co-players "trustworthiness"; cf. Wang et al., 2015)  could be used to estimate the generative 357 
model of depressed patients and systematically compare it to the model of healthy controls.  In 358 
clinical practice, the availability of behavioral data throughout the diagnostic process could 359 
provide clinicians with a concrete idea of their patient's implicit internal models of their social 360 
environment. This could be exploited, again, to individualize therapies. 361 
Our proposed research agenda is in line with a broader trend away from fixed clinical 362 
categories towards a more multidimensional perspective on diagnosing mental disorders (Insel 363 
et al., 2010). Multidimensional approaches can account for heterogeneity in patient populations 364 
and shed light on shared mechanisms in what have been assumed to be distinct disorders. In the 365 
future, computational markers of depression may prove to be beneficial in arriving at a precise 366 
description and selecting optimal treatment strategies. These formalizations could objectify the 367 
diagnostics of mental disorders in general, and strengthen research on shared mechanisms. 368 
From a methodological perspective, future research should ‚Äì as the first step ‚Äì specify 369 
several prototypes of generative models that describe the active inference of social contexts in 370 
healthy vs. clinical samples, as well as between different "phenotypes" of the disorders under 371 
investigation. These models should be guided by theories regarding different etiologies of each 372 
disorder. As the second step, these generative models could serve to simulate behavior, and 373 
AN ACTIVE INFERENCE ACCOUNT TO SOCIAL INFERENCE IN DEPRESSION 
then matche d to empirical data from healthy and clinical samples. For example, parameter 374 
estimates could be obtained from (social) decision behavior (Smith et al., 2022b)  in standard 375 
experimental paradigms (e.g., game theoretical tasks, Wang et al., 2015) . In the final step, 376 
researchers should identify those generative models that yield the best model fit and work on 377 
interventions to optimize their parameters with respect to mental health.  378 
Finally, another important step would be to develop a model applied to describe the 379 
interaction of multiple agents with different generative POMDP models. This would enable 380 
researchers to study dynamic interaction patterns in health and disease, and their effects on the 381 
corresponding generative model of the agents involved.  382 
6. Conclusion 383 
There is broad consensus that social functioning, i.e., the anticipation, perception, and 384 
processing of social information, as well a s social behavior, is impaired in depression. We 385 
attribute these impairments primarily to deviations in patients' internal models of their social 386 
environment, leading to biased inferences about social contexts in the present. Formalizing 387 
these biases within the active inference framework and operationalizing them in the context of 388 
experimental paradigms that involve social inferences (e.g., socioeconomic decision tasks) can 389 
shed light on the wide heterogeneity of depressive symptomatology and provide insigh ts into 390 
the underlying mechanisms and configurations of patients' internal models of their social world. 391 
Determining the generative model of patients in social contexts would improve both diagnostics 392 
and research, as well as facilitate treatment selection for mental disorders. 393
