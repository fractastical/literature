Abstract
In decision-making, choices impact the present and cascade into future decisions,
highlighting the importance of confidence when making a decision. Here, we
investigated the meta-network level neural correlates of this confidence by estimating
the instantaneous changes in precision in selecting actions and comparing them with
brain state trajectories. To confirm the relationship between behavioral and neural
signals, we leveraged inter-subject correlation to determine how similar the group-shared
components of time-series of precision-triggered meta-network occurrences are across
participants and how this similarity changes during decision-making. We found that
policy precision is a proper behavioral signal to explain the meta-network level neural
dynamics. It positively correlated with the default mode network (DMN)-dominant
state, the occurrence of which is mutually exclusive with the dorsal attentional network-
(DAN) and frontoparietal network (FPN)-dominant state, the activation of which is
speculated to be associated with a highly uncertain state and arises from increased
integration between the DAN, FPN, and DMN. Therefore, supporting the novel
perspective that the DMN may reflect internal beliefs, these findings indicate that their
integration promotes the DAN and FPN to exert attention to decrease uncertainty.

The active inference framework, unifying the theory of action and perception, 13
attempts to elucidate a series of decision-making by minimizing a unified objective 14
function, free energy. This framework treats action selection as an inference problem15
as well. Therefore, not only the belief about states but also the belief aboutpolicies 16
are inferred, and the precision of this belief was suggested [16]. With this framework17
(Fig. 1b), the agent infers both its current state (sn) and optimal policies (Ï€) based on 18
the probability of both outcomes in each context (A) and transitions (B) depending on19
the policy. Given its prior preferences over outcomes (C), the expected free energy (G)20
of each policy is determined based on how closely the expected future outcomes align to21
preferred outcomes by following each policy, which is weighted by the precision over the22
policies (Î³), indicating how confident the agent is that these policies will minimize G (for23
the details of the G calculation, please see SI Appendix, Supplementary Methods) [15].24
Precision over the policies has been suggested to be encoded by dopamine [1,18,39];25
however, the exact computational role of dopamine remains controversial [4,19,21,38].26
This controversy is attributed to two main reasons: first, dopaminergic neurons project27
to the whole brain, and the brain operates through highly complex and interconnected28
networks. Therefore, observing local and regional activation is insufficient, and 29
understanding its pathways and subsequent effects through connectivity is 30
crucial [2,41,42]. Second, anycomputational modelof a biological signal remains 31
hypothetical by nature [11,14]. We argue that if a newly proposed signal can explain32
network-level dynamics in the context of a series of decision-making, it could at least33
validate the role of the signal in the neural process. This should precede further detailed34
studies on the hypothetical behavioral signals. 35

Discussion 269
The findings of this study can be summarized as follows: First, policy precision is an270
important signal to explain the meta-network level changes in the brain. Second, the271
uncertainty of prediction (prediction precision) increases the integration between the272
DMN and the other networks, including the DAN and FPN, which may relate to 273
occurrences of state 3, where the DAN and FPN are dominant. Lastly, when 274
participants change their action selections by observations that are against their belief275
states, integration between the DMN and VAN increases. Subsequently, integrations276
between the VAN and LIM network as well as the DAN and SOM network increase and277
the recruitment of the FPN decreases until the changed action is taken. 278
The dISC time-series of the occurrence probability of state 3 was higher when the 279
occurrence probability of state 3 increased. The original time-series analysis showed 280
that, when there was insufficient evidence (at the beginning of each task) or feedback281
that did not meet expectations (causing incongruent outcome in the subsequent trial by282
changing oneâ€™s choice), that is, uncertain feedback, state 3 was primarily active. In state283
3, the DAN and FPN are predominantly activated, and these networks are involved in284
executive function and goal-oriented, cognitively demanding tasks [26, 46]. Based on our285
results, we speculate that the core functions of the DAN and FPN are related to 286
exerting attentional efforts in an uncertain state where precision cannot be sufficiently287
high. To determine how state 3 is activated according to uncertainty, we investigated288
the connectivity between brain regions that is supposed to lead to the activation of289
meta-network states. Most of the integration, including the DMN, was higher with 290
uncertain than with certain feedback. 291
In addition, state 2, where the DMN was dominantly activated, and integrations, 292
including the FPN, especially with the DMN, were the lowest, was significantly 293
positively correlated with the policy precision signal. Although the DMN is generally294
regarded as a resting network activated during rest, mind-wandering, and self-referential295
tasks [9,10,32], enhanced suggestions explaining the function of the DMN focusing on296
intrinsic information rather than just resting or doing nothing, have been 297
raised [5,30,33]. It has been suggested that the DMN detects the associative relevance298
of internal and external stimuli and enables value coding [35]. From this viewpoint, an299
alternative proposal argues that understanding the self-referential function of the DMN300
entails linking it to the choice of the best actions within the environmental context,301
necessitating recursive exploration. [22]. Following this suggestion, there is a concept302

processes and required integration that normally increases in uncertain feedback. 339

Participants 352
Fifty-four volunteers participated in this study (15 female and 39 male; mean [standard353
deviation] age was 22.4 [3.2] years; all individuals were right-handed and had no history354
of psychiatric or neurological disorders). All participants provided written informed 355

fMRI Data Acquisition and Analysis 360
All participants underwent MRI using a 3.0-T MRI scanner (Siemens Verio Syngo).361
Both T1-weighted (T1w; echo time [TE] = 2.02 ms, repetition time [TR] = 2400 ms,362
field of view (FOV) = 224Ã— 224 mm, and 0.7 mm iso-voxel) and T2-weighted (T2w;363
TE = 330 ms, TR = 2200 ms, FOV = 224Ã— 224 mm, and 0.7 mm iso-voxel) structural364
images were acquired. A total of 240 echo-planar imaging scans of BOLD responses365
(multiband factor = 4, TE = 32 ms, TR = 1.5 s, flip angle = 50Â°, FOV = 225Ã— 221 366
mm, matrix size = 110Ã— 107, 2.0 mm iso-voxel with no gap, 64 slices, phase encoding367
= anterior to posterior) were performed using a 32-channel head coil. Field map images368
(TR = 731 ms, TE: 4.92 ms and 7.38 ms, flip angle = 90Â°) were also acquired to correct369
distorted images. In every trial, the participants performed the experimental task with370
both hands and pressed an MRI-compatible button to indicate their decision. In the371
pretraining session, prior to the first scan, the participants were acquainted with the372
apparatus and tasks were performed during scanning. The sequences executed during373
this pretraining session were not encountered later in the experiment. For details about374
the fMRI data preprocessing, please see SI Appendix, Supplementary Methods. 375
After preprocessing, we acquired the averaged region of interest (ROI) time-series376
extracted from the regions from the Schaefer Atlas [37], including 100 regions covering377
the cortex, and the Melbourne Subcortex Atlas [44] covering the subcortex. In addition,378
to include other subcortical regions related to important neurotransmitters, including379
dopamine and norepinephrine, we added some regions from the CIT168 atlas [29] and380
Harvard Ascending Arousal Network Atlas [13] to obtain signals from a total of 147381
ROIs (SI Appendix, Tables S9). 382
To investigate the dynamic changes in different levels of neural interactions, we 383
obtained neural signals at the network and edge levels. At the edge level, we used phase384
coherence to calculate the connectivity between two regions. This method is adept at385
capturing temporally delayed relationships and exhibits high sensitivity in detecting 386
ultraslow oscillatory processes that underlie the formation of RSNs, a phenomenon 387
corroborated by recent experimental and computational research [12,31]. In our study,388
we used a recently developed data-driven approach named Leading Eigenvector 389
Dynamics Analysis that can reduce the dimensionality by considering only the relative390
phase of BOLD signals and capturing instantaneous phase-locking (PL) patterns [6,48].391
However, in this study, we did not employ k-means clustering to identify representative392
states based on the leading eigenvector. Instead, we used the outer product of the 393
leading eigenvectors of the PL patterns to reconstruct the connectivity patterns at each394
time point. K-means clustering was not used as it does not consider the graph-based395
connectivity of brain structures that generate signals when distinguishing clusters. 396
Therefore, to extract the representation of the overall brain state corresponding to397
network-level interactions, we employed the GLMM, an approach that involves directly398
estimating the graph structure that generates the signals, which serves as an alternative399
