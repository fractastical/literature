Background. Interpersonal problems are common in mental disorders like depression and social anxiety 9 
disorder. However, there is a lack of formal models to explain idiosyncrasies in patients' interpersonal 10 
functioning. Following modern computational accounts of perception and action, interpersonal experi-11 
ence and behavior results from implicit inference about hidden environmental properties. 12 
Methods. We simulate interpersonal decision-making in a â€œtrust gameâ€ using a POMDP generative 13 
model and active inference. Simulated agents decide to either keep or invest an initial budget to a trustee. 14 
If the agents invests, the trustee can cooperate  with or exploit the agentâ€™s trust. Agents perform active 15 
inference of the context (cooperative vs. hostile) and action selection. By introducing systematic biases 16 
to the generative model, we simulate several subtypes of depression and social anxiety.  We then col-17 
lected data of N=25 patients with diagnosed depressive disorders and fit the generative model to the 18 
data.  19

Introduction 2 
Impairments in interpersonal experience and behavior are highly prevalent in mental disorders and can 3 
cause tremendous suffering and social disadvantages (social anxiety disorder: Alden & Taylor, 2004; 4 
eating disorders: Arcelus et al., 2013; schizophrenia: Hooley, 2010; major depression: Kupferberg et al., 5 
2016; borderline personality disorder: Lazarus et al., 2014; post-traumatic stress disorder: Scoglio et al., 6 
2022). Determining whether interpersonal impairments are the cause or consequence of a mental health 7 
problem is challenging. It is likely that longitudinal bidirectional relationships exist (Beeson et al., 2020; 8 
Kirchner et al., 2022). Impairments in interpersonal experience and behavior predict important clinical 9 
outcomes and play a role in the development and maintenance of mental disorder (Backs-Dermott et al., 10 
2010; Bell et al., 2018; Kirchner et al., 2022; Quilty et al., 2013; Tokuda et al., 2008; Woods et al., 2021). 11 
Upon closer examination, interpersonal symptoms tend to share significant similarities across diagnostic 12 
categories.  For instance, social withdrawal is common in depressed (FernÃ¡ndez-Theoduloz et al., 2019), 13 
and socially anxious individuals (Hudson & Rapee, 2000) ; but also in individuals with chronic pain 14 
(Harris, 2014) , post -traumatic stress disorder (Maercker & Horn, 2013) , or borderline personality 15 
disorder (Liebke et al., 2017) . It follows that a  breadth of different underlying causes may result in  16 
commonalities at the level  of observable behavior  (Gao et al., 2017) . These commonalities are of 17 
particular interest in light of generally high comorbidity rates among mental disorders (Conway et al., 18 
2022; Cuthbert & Insel, 2013; Dalgleish et al., 2020). However, meaningful differences in interpersonal 19 
experience and behavior can still be identified between- and within diagnostic categories. Joormann & 20 
Gotlib (2006) demonstrated differences between depressed and socially anxious individuals in terms of 21 
sensitivity to social stimuli. Also within the spectrum of depressive disorders , different â€œinterpersonal 22 
subtypesâ€ can be identified (e.g., â€œvindictiveâ€ vs. â€œsocially avoidantâ€, Simon et al., 2015). 23 
There is a lack of formal theories or models that can effectively integrate and accurately operationalize 24 
the differences and commonalities in patients' interpersonal experiences and behaviors  across- and 25 
within disorders. We have previously described the theoretical structure and functional form of such a 26 
model (Kirchner et al., 2022) , where we conceptualized impairments in interpersonal experience and 27 
behavior as the result of biased implicit inferences about hidden properties (â€œstatesâ€) of the environment. 28 
Individual biases can stem from, for example, divergent probability estimates about the environment, 29 
differences in evaluating  interpersonal observations  or the  advantageousness attributed  to these 30 
observations. 31 
Promises of Computational Approaches in Mental Health 32 
The promise of applying formal, normative models to mental health conditions is to identify distinct 33 
behavioral or neural phenotypes, or computational assays (Karvelis et al., 2023; Montague et al., 2012). 34 
Formal computational models of clinical phenotypes, coupled with empirical data,  may open novel 35 
avenues for researching heterogeneity , developing personalized treatments  and predicting treatment 36 
outcomes (Schwartenbeck & Friston, 2016) . Bayesian accounts of behavior have gained substantial 37 
4 
 
momentum in psychiatry and clinical psychology, reframing mental disorders as caused by aberrant  2 
implicit inference (Pio-Lopez et al., 2022) . Bayesian inference has been applied to a wide range of 3 
mental disorders, including schizophrenia (Fletcher & Frith, 2009; Haarsma et al., 2022)  and autism 4 
(Palmer et al., 2017; Pellicano & Burr, 2012), panic disorder (Maisto et al., 2021), social anxiety disorder 5 
(Gerrans & Murray, 2020), post-traumatic stress disorder ( Linson & Friston, 2019) , anorexia nervosa 6 
(Barca & Pezzulo, 2020), and depression (Barrett et al., 2016; M. Berg et al., 2022). While conceptually 7 
attractive, many theoretical accounts suffer from two crucial limitations. First, they tend to be developed 8 
within the boundaries of a specific, categorical diagnosis. Research has shown repeatedly that 9 
traditional, categorical nosologies show limited predictive power, arbitrary boundaries between sub -10 
clinical and clinical expressions of a trait or symptom, and high levels of  comorbidity (Kotov et al., 11 
2018). Developments such as the HiToP (Kotov et al., 2018)  or RDoC (Cuthbert & Insel, 2013)  are 12 
moving away from a description of mental disorders by a set of distinct diagnostic categories  and 13 
towards dimensional perspectives on clinical symptoms and syndromes . Secondly, many studies lack 14 
rigorous mathematical operationalizations that allow for com puter simulations and a thorough formal 15 
evaluation of theoretical assumptions considering data. The Active Inference framework may help 16 
address these shortcomings, providing flexible and tractable algorithms for inference (Parr et al., 2022). 17 
Active Inference â€“ A Framework to Model Mental Disorders 18 
Active Inference is a theoretical account of perception, action and learning in complex systems (Friston 19 
et al., 2016; Heins et al., 2022; Parr et al., 2022). It is commonly used to model decision-making under 20 
uncertainty in cognitive neuroscience, but its application now extend to a wide range of fields and 21 
disciplines, including clinical psychology and psychiatry (Benrimoh et al., 2018; Montague et al., 2012; 22 
Smith et al., 2021). Complex behavior under Active Inference results from the imperative to maximize 23 
a lower bound on Bayesian model evidence, or minimize variational free energy (VFE). In other words, 24 
Active Inference ensures that incoming sensory information is well -explained by an internal, formal 25 
model of the world.  To effectively navigate and interact with the (interpersonal) environment, agents 26 
need to infer its current state and decide what actions to pursue to resolve uncertainty about the 27 
environment and obtain preferred observations and outcomes. To accomplish this, it is crucial for the 28 
brain to entertain a generative model of its surroundings . A generative model  is a formal 29 
conceptualization of the processes that generate sensory observations (Friston et al., 2016) . The 30 
generative model is used to form predictions, such as expectations about an interpersonal environment, 31 
which are based on sensory information and prior beliefs. Deviations between sensory information and 32 
model predictions can be used to update the generative model. This iterative process ensures that 33 
predictions continue to minimize VFE over time. To achieve the optimal balance between trusting prior 34 
beliefs or the current sensory information, their respective precision needs to be estimated (Brown et al., 35 
2013). The process of minimizing VFE is thought to underlie perception. Beyond perception, Active 36 
Inference offers a solution for action selection that dynamically interacts with VFE -based perceptual 37 
inference. Desirable states or outcomes (preferences) are part of the agentâ€™s generative model and can 38 
5 
 
be reframed as a type of prior prediction (Da Costa et al., 2020). Actions are selected in a way that makes 2 
these predictions more probable. In Active Inference, actions hence ensure that preferred outcomes or 3 
states are reached or maintained. Action selection requires minimizing the expected free energy (EFE) 4 
ğ¸ over future states, given a sequence of actions. Policies that lead to preferred outcomes are more likely 5 
to be pursued by the agent. Agents are hence equipped to choose proximal actions that lead to the 6 
achievement of a distal goal state, i.e., planning -as-inference. Through this, Active Inference offers a 7 
computational strategy to solve the explore -exploit dilemma (Heins et al., 2022) . In Active Inference, 8 
the state of the environment is usually modelled as unobservable, or hidden, to the agent. The agent has 9 
to infer the hidden state from sensory observations, which are generated by the hidden state in a 10 
probabilistic manner (Da Costa et al., 2020) . An agent further needs a representation of how, 11 
probabilistically, their own actions alter environmental states. This ensures that only such actions are 12 
chosen that either resolve the agentâ€™s uncertainty (explore)  or bring the agent closer to their preferred 13 
outcome (exploit). Partially observable Markov decision processes (POMDPs) have proved to be a 14 
convenient choice for generative models in Active Inference (Da Costa et al., 2020; Heins et al., 2022). 15 
POMDPs model the environment in terms of hidden states, which change dynamically over time as a 16 
function of the current state and the agentâ€™s actions. 17 
To summarize, agents perpetually infer the real-world causes of incoming sensory observations. Agents 18 
will choose policies, or action sequences, that resolve uncertainty and  maximize the likelihood of 19 
achieving preferred states. To do this, agents need to estimate how observations relate to hidden states, 20 
how hidden states evolve over time, and how their actions impact the evolution of hidden states.  21 
Neuroeconomic Decision-making and Interpersonal Behavior in Mental Disorders.  22 
Neuroeconomic games are complex and strategic, yet controlled experiments (Glimcher & Rustichini, 23 
2004; Robson et al., 2020) that have been utilized to study interpersonal decision-making in both healthy 24 
participants and clinical populations (King-Casas & Chiu, 2012). In the present work, we focus on trust 25 
games, where an agent needs to decide whether to keep or invest a small monetary wage with a trustee. 26 
If the agent decides to invest, the trustee can decide to cooperate, usually resulting in an increased payout 27 
for the agent ; or defect, resulting in a loss for the agent. Previous research suggests idiosyncrasies in 28 
gameplay behavior in mental disorders (King-Casas & Chiu, 2012; Robson et al., 2020) . In particular, 29 
gameplay behavior in individuals with depression and social anxiety disorder is well documented (Ong 30 
et al., 2017; C. Sripada et al., 2013; C. S. Sripada et al., 2009; Zhang et al., 2012) . Individuals with 31 
remitted major depressive disorder tend to defect less when in the role of the trustee compared to healthy 32 
controls, which has been related to a reduced ability to cope with the socio -emotional demands of the 33 
trust game (Ong et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2012) . In social anxiet y, functional imaging data suggests 34 
decreased processing of the opponentâ€™s motivation (C. S. Sripada et al., 2009)  and decreased neural 35 
responses to a cooperative trustee compared to controls (C. Sripada et al., 2013). Additionally, decision-36 
making in neuroeconomic gameplay has been associated with important clinical variables. In an 37 
6 
 
exemplary study, individuals with a history of high-lethality suicide attempts were less sensitive to the 2 
costs of punishing their partner in a trust game compared to patients with a history of low -lethality 3 
suicide attempts (Szanto et al., 2014). Hence, interpersonal decision-making in gameplay allows us to 4 
model clinically relevant experiences and behavior (Hinterbuchinger et al., 2018; King-Casas & Chiu, 5 
2012; Robson et al., 2020; Tzieropoulos, 2013; Wang et al., 2015). 6 
Active Inference in Interpersonal Decision-making 7 
Interpersonal contexts are highly complex and recursive. An individual needs to constantly monitor the 8 
dynamic actions of another individual, and predict future actions based on prior experiences with this 9 
individual or situation to choose appropriate and timely reactions. Moutoussis and colleagues (201 4) 10 
equip simulated agents with  variational Bayesian message passing  and engage in a trust game. They 11 
demonstrate that the agents are able to iteratively update beliefs about an opponent and make decisions 12 
that maximize reward over time (Moutoussis et al., 2014). Similarly, Schwartenbeck et al. (2015) model 13 
behavior and neural activity in a neuroeconomic â€œlimited offerâ€ game using Active Inference. 14 
Participants had to decide whether they would accept a lower offer or wait for a higher one, at the risk 15 
of losing any offers if they waited for too long. Active Inference showed superior performance compared 16 
to classical utility maximization schemes in explaining participant behavior (Schwartenbeck et al., 17 
2015). Finally, Cullen and colleagues (2018) simulated agents equipped with Active Inference in an 18 
artificial game-play environment and demonstrate that agents are capable of forming representations of 19 
their environments that help guide research into neurobiological underpinnings of mental disorder  20 
(Cullen et al., 2018). 21

We use a classical economic game to simulate interpersonal decision-making in depression (see Figure 4 
1A). In the trust game (J. Berg et al., 1995; King -Casas & Chiu, 2012) , the agentâ€™s task is to play a 5 
multi-round investment-return game. In our simplified version, the agent receives a small amount of 6 
money in each trial, which they can either keep to themselves or invest their budget with a trustee. The 7 
artificial trustee can either keep or return half the multiplied wage invested by the agent. If the trustee 8 
decides to return half of the investment (return), the investment is multiplied by three, and split evenly 9 
between agent and trustee, leading to a surplus reward created by the joint venture (i.e., the agent invests 10 
0.5 and receives 0.75). If, in contrast, the trustee decides to keep the agentâ€™s investment to themselves 11 
(defect), the agent loses  all their budget to the trustee . In other words, if the agent invests, he can 12 
encounter both a return of investment (win) or a loss of their investment (in case the trustee defects). In 13 
the simulation, there were two different interpersonal contexts (Figure 1B). In the cooperative context, 14 
the trustee collaborates (i.e., shares the agentâ€™s investment) with a probability of ğ‘=0.8. In the 15 
exploitative, or hostile context, the trustee tends to keep the agentâ€™s investment to themselves and shares 16 
with a probability of ğ‘=0.2. We simulated two games: a cooperative -to-hostile game, where a 17 
cooperative phase (t=20) was followed by a hostile phase (t=20), and a hostile -to-cooperative game 18 
(t=20 cooperative trials, followed by t=20 hostile trials) . To maximize rewards over time, the agent 19 
should frequently share with the trustee in cooperative contexts and keep their initial wage in the hostile 20

Discussion 2 
In this study, we repor t a series of simulations in which an artificial agent engages in a classical trust 3 
game and fit this model to behavioral data from outpatients with depression playing the game . Using 4 
Active Inference, the agents infer the interpersonal context from the trusteeâ€™s behavior. We developed 5 
agents in which several biases  are combined  to simulate depression and social anxiety  and finally 6 
inverted the model onto behavioral data of patients to retrieve individually optimal parameters of the 7 
generative model.  8 
In simulations, we demonstrate d how the combination of different parameter perturbations may 9 
approximate behavioral strategies of individuals with social anxiety disorder and depression. Depression 10 
here resulted from the  combination of outcome devaluation and pessimistic prior beliefs ( reward-11 
insensitive and pessimistic ), or rigid beliefs about context dynamics in combination with pessimistic 12 
priors (fatalistic). Social anxiety disorder resulted from an increased risk aversion in combination with 13 
a high prior belief to be in a random, unpredictable interpersonal context  (insecure-avoidant) or an 14 
increased loss avoidance in combination with pessimistic prior- and state transition beliefs (trauma-and-15 
defeat).   16 
Pessimistic priors and fatalistic beliefs about the temporal dynamics of hidden states were at the core of 17 
the depressed agentsâ€™ generative models. In line with this, individuals with depression show a variety of 18 
pessimistic interpretation biases (Everaert et al., 2017) , pessimistic tendencies such as learned 19 
helplessness and low self-efficacy (Seligman, 1972) and potentially a lack of interpersonal skills (Segrin, 20 
2000). A lack of reward valuation and pessimistic prior beliefs may also relate to a reduced motivation 21 
to engage with their environment in patients (FernÃ¡ndez-Theoduloz et al., 2019). In the trust game, the 22 
depressed agents earned significantly less compared to healthy agents during a hostile-to-cooperative 23 
series. However, the depressed agents outcompete the healthy agent in a cooperative-to-hostile series. 24 
Here, the depressed agents switch to an avoidant behavioral strategy soon after the context switch. The 25 
healthy agentâ€™s optimistic beliefs about their environment and their strong motivation to win a joint 26 
reward keeps them from adopting an avoidant strategy, even after observing a series of defects. In the 27 
trust game, avoiding the trusteeâ€™s decision is an intelligent strategy in a hostile context. However, this 28 
strategy comes at the expense of potentially missing a switch to a cooperative context, explaining why 29 
the depressed agents earn significantly less compared to the healthy agent in the hostile-to-cooperative 30 
series. Our simulation supports the idea that in some environments, depression may be an adaptive 31 
response that protects the individual from exploitation (Prins et al., 2015) . However, the optimistic 32 
(healthy) agent is best equipped to flexibly navigate dynamically changing contexts over longer time 33 
periods, as indicated by a simulation with more volatile context dynamics (appendix 2). Meanwhile, 34 
individuals with depression or anxiety may show less flexible behavior as a consequence of e.g. learned 35 
helplessness (Seligman, 1972) , aberrant reward processing (Lewinsohn, 1974)  or cognitive 36 
immunization (Rief & Joormann, 2019) . Further, some research suggests that individuals with 37 
44 
 
depression show a more realistic interpretation of action-outcome contingencies (depressive realism, 2 
Dunning & Story, 1991; Moore & Fresco, 2012) . In line with this, the depressed agent s adapt their 3 
posterior beliefs over contexts more quickly and accurately and their behavioral strategy switched 4 
quicker in hostile contexts, compared to the more optimistic agent in our simulation. 5 
Depressive symptoms often manifest episodically, with patients experiencing alternating phases of 6 
severe and relatively mild symptoms . In Active Inference, episodic courses may be a function of the 7 
transition model (or ğµ matrix). If individuals believe for example that both hostile and cooperative 8 
contexts are relatively stable, then a few negative interpersonal triggers may be sufficient for the agent 9 
to infer a stable, hostile state, which can be updated through repeated exposure to positive interpersonal 10 
exchanges.  11 
The â€œsocially anxiousâ€ agents focused on risk aversion and showed increased avoidance behaviors. 12 
Avoidance is a defining feature of social anxiety disorder (BÃ¶gels & Mansell, 2004) , which could be 13 
mediated through the combination of loss aversion and pessimistic prior beliefs in Active Inference. The 14 
distinction between the two subtypes of social anxiety disorder lies in their prior beliefs about initial 15 
states, the temporal dynamics of these states, and the degree to which they are motivated to achieve a 16 
positive outcome (i.e., observing a successful collaboration vs. keeping the initial budget and not taking 17 
the risk ). The avoidance behavior of the  insecure-avoidant socially anxious agent is driven by the 18 
insecure belief that the environment is random  and unpredictable. In contrast, t he trauma-and-defeat 19 
agent avoids collaborating with the trustee because they hold strong beliefs to be in a hostile 20 
environment. The two agents are similar with respect to their behavioral strategies and earned rewards, 21 
but the differences in model perturbations may be of crucial importance during treatment. It can be 22 
conjectured that cognitive restructuring and social competences training may be the most beneficial to  23 
insecure-avoidant agents, while, for example, interpersonal trauma may need addressing in trauma-and-24 
defeat-type patients first.  25 
In the Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology (HiToP, Kotov et al., 2018), social anxiety disorder 26 
and depression are part of the internalizing spectrum, but while social anxiety disorder loads on the 27 
â€œfearâ€ subfactor, depression loads on the â€œdistressâ€ subfactor. In terms of computational transdiagnostic 28 
processes, this difference may lie in the preference over outcomes, or the ğ¶ vector. In one of our models 29 
for depression ( reward-insensitive and pessimistic ), social anhedonia is modelled via  a reduced 30 
preference for positive social rewards. Social anxiety disorder, in contrast, is a function of high levels 31 
of risk avoidance, where a disproportionately large weight is placed on risk and loss avoidance. 32 
Fitting our model to the  behavioral data of patients with diagnosed depressive disorder of varying 33 
symptom severity yielded optimal values for all parameters of the generative model ğ´,ğµ,ğ¶,ğ· and 34 
weighting parameter  ğœ€. We considered the resulting generative model parameters separately for 35 
individuals with high- and low symptom severity and the game sequence they played. Both symptom 36 
severity and game sequence affected the parameters of the generative model in a subtle way. The 37 
45 
 
observation model ğ´ did not differ between individuals with low vs. high symptom severity, indicating 2 
that both groups did not differ in their ability to relate a specific outcome to the corresponding context. 3 
The transition dynamics ğµ differed very subtly. More severe symptoms were associated with an 4 
increased belief in the stability of the cooperative context, and less stability of the hostile context. 5 
Furthermore, higher symptom severity was associated with a slightly increased belief of transitioning 6 
into a hostile context when the current context was cooperative, and a decreased belief in a transition 7 
from a hostile into a cooperative context. Outcome preferences ğ¶ did not differ between median -split 8 
patient groups. When considering individuals with minimum- and maximum symptom severity, a slight 9 
tendency emerged, suggesting higher symptom severity is associated with lower utility assigned to the 10 
social reward (return on investment), slightly more risk aversion, and higher preference for keeping their 11 
budget compared to individuals with milder symptoms. Counter -intuitively, more severe depressive 12 
symptoms were associated with a heightened prior belief (ğ·) to be in a cooperative context, and a lower 13 
belief to be in a hostile context. Finally, weighting parameter ğœ€ was introduced to govern the weighting 14 
of epistemic vs. hedonic drives in the generative model. While ğœ€ differed inter-individually, this variance 15 
was not related to symptom severity.  16 
When equipping the agent -model with the optimized parameters derived from patient data, we could 17 
simulate the patientâ€™s behavior over several games. We identified idiosyncrasies in the patientâ€™s 18 
respective decision-making, with mildly depressed patients forming more precise posterior beliefs over 19 
the cooperative context, whereas more severely depressed individuals formed higher posterior beliefs 20 
over the hostile context. Our approach demonstrates that data -inspired agent models can be useful in 21 
simulating patient behavior efficiently for a large number of games and different contexts.  22 
Limitations and future research 23 
The model presented here provides starting points for explaining heterogeneity in social experience and 24 
behavior among individuals with mental health problems. For the simulations , the parameter 25 
specifications were chosen so that they align with the literature, but are ultimately based on theoretical 26 
considerations. By fitting the model to behavioral data of patients with depression, we show that 27 
parameter distributions can slightly differ between individuals with low- vs. high symptom severity. Of 28 
note, these results need to be interpreted with caution. An important goal of the optimization procedure 29 
was to demonstrate its feasibility in the context of relatively sparse behavioral data and complex 30 
generative models such as POMDPs. We cannot yet determine if these changes in model parameters are 31 
unique to depression or similar to those in, e.g., social anxiety , and whether they can account for 32 
substantial variance when comparing clinical with non -clinical samples . More data from diverse 33 
diagnostic categories (ICD -10 social anxiety, depression) or symptom spectra (HiTOP internalizing 34 
spectrum, subfactors fear vs. distress) is required to draw conclusions about the specificity of parameter 35 
changes. A limitation is the relatively small sample size, and the lack of a non-clinical control group in 36 
the dataset used for model fitting. Future work should aim at collecting data from both diagnosed- and 37 
46 
 
undiagnosed individuals to represent the entire spectrum from mental well-being- to disorder. On the 2 
other hand, heterogeneity in model parameters emerged despite the lack of a non-clinical control group, 3 
and the boundaries between non-clinical sadness in the general population and depressive disorders are 4 
fluent (Tebeka et al., 2021). 5 
Of note, finding clear and distinct computational phenotypes within a diagnostic category has proven to 6 
be extremely challenging (Karvelis et al., 2023 ; Robson et al., 2020) . The test-retest reliability of the 7 
generative model parameters identified in this task is rather unclear. We nonetheless argue that there is 8 
value in understanding how changes to the parameters of a generative model  influence behavior to 9 
inform etiological concepts, instead of basing computational phenotypes on nosological categories. In 10 
case transdiagnostic affiliations can be derived from computational models of behavior, there is still 11 
very limited research into the reliability and validity of computational markers. A recent meta-analysis 12 
suggests that cognitive tasks and computational markers derived from task behavior shows relatively 13 
poor psychometric properties (Karvelis et al., 2023) . Longitudinal task batteries may be  necessary to 14 
equip researchers with the data to disentangle measurement errors, practice effects and state- and trait-15 
like changes in behavior. Further empirical validations of our model may need to consider multiple 16 
experiments of interpersonal behavior in tasks with varying degrees of similarity.  17 
Conclusion 18 
We simulated complex interpersonal experiences and behaviors in mental disorders as Active Inference 19 
of interpersonal context within a neuroeconomic game. We fit our agent model on behavioral data of 20 
participants with diagnosed depressive disorders  of varying severity. Our model allows for the 21 
simulation of complex interpersonal experiences and behaviors, and enables the operationalization of 22 
transdiagnostic and disorder -relevant processes, as well as heterogeneity in social functioning across 23 
individuals. Fitting the model to behavioral data can provide more mechanistic insights into biased 24 
expectations and assumptions in clinical populations, with implications for personalized diagnosis and 25 
treatment. 26 
Our simulations yield three fundamental insight s: (1) different generative models of the environment 27 
can result in similar interpersonal strategies, (2) avoidant interpersonal behavior s are adaptive  and 28 
functionally sensible in exploitative environments  and (3) exploration, which appears to be a crucial 29 
characteristic of healthy interpersonal behavior, can lead to losses in some environments; however, on 30 
average, it produces the most robust outcome rates in dynamically changing environments. Developing 31 
computational phenotypes based on observable behaviors is an important cornerstone of personalized 32 
diagnostics and tailored interventions.  By fitting our model to the behavioral data of patients, we can 33 
obtain approximations to the computations underlying patientâ€™s behavioral strategies.  Neuroeconomic 34 
games offer a relatively naturalistic, yet well-controlled experimental framework. Future research needs 35 
to further provide empirical validation of these ideas, ideally within longitudinal, multi-task studies.  36

Discussion: transdiagnostic processes 2 
In the first agent, we targeted the observation model ( ğ´, observational uncertainty), by decreasing the 3 
certainty with which observations are connected to a specific  context. As a consequence, the  agent 4 
struggled making precise context inferences. Biases to the observation model could be related to 5 
â€œcognitive immunizationâ€ techniques, where a friendly interaction is connected to a hostile context 6 
(Kube, 2023; Panitz et al., 2021; Rief & Joormann, 2019) . This thought pattern is frequently observed 7 
in patients with depression or social anxiety (Everaert et al., 2017; Rief et al., 2022) . The inability to 8 
draw â€œcorrectâ€ conclusions from a given observation may  also be the consequence of a lack of soci o-9 
emotional skills, a common impairment in clinical populations (Nestor et al., 2022; Segrin, 2000; 10 
Wolkenstein et al., 2011) . Many patients  further connect positive interpersonal experiences to 11 
randomness rather than true benevolence (Ewen et al., 2022). In conclusion, perturbations to the agentâ€™s 12 
ğ´ matrix have the consequence that observations do not have a systematic or sensible effect on state 13 
inference. 14 
Next, we perturbed the agentâ€™s transition parameter ( ğµ), introducing fatalistic beliefs. The transition 15 
matrix comprises beliefs about the evolution of contexts over time and the  agentâ€™s action-dependent 16 
state transitions . Believing that a hostile context will likely develop into a cooperative one is a 17 
computational approximation to the optimism bias in healthy adults (Bracha & Brown, 2012; Sharot, 18 
2011). In contrast, believing that hostile contexts are the norm while cooperative contexts are futile is in 19 
line with e.g., hopelessness (Liu et al., 2015), learned helplessness (Seligman, 1972), low self-efficacy 20 
(Maier & Seligman, 2016; Niu et al., 2022), or interpersonal distrust (Kim et al., 2012). Empirical data 21 
suggests that individuals with depression and social anxiety show deficits in social cognition, 22 
mentalization and theory of mind (Baker & Edelmann, 2002; Nestor et al., 2022) , which may be a 23 
consequence of  inaccurate beliefs about the dynamics of interpersonal situations  â€“ for example, 24 
believing that interpersonal situations will inevitably end in hostility  (LÃ¼dtke & Westermann, 2023; 25 
Segrin, 2000). Supporting the relevance of expectations, recent data suggest that negative expectations 26 
drive subclinical depression to a larger degree than actual negative experiences (LÃ¼dtke & Westermann, 27 
2023). Furthermore, biases in the transition model ğµ may be an appropriate operationalization for altered 28 
future thinking in depression. A recent meta -analysis suggests a link between depression and the 29 
difficulty to imagine specific positive events in the future , which may reduce motivation to engage in 30 
any activities, especially given that the ability to imagine negative future events is unaltered in 31 
depression (Gamble et al., 2019). 32 
Gaining a joint win is the preferred outcome in healthy individuals. To simulate psychopathological 33 
behavior, we manipulated the ğ¶ parameter such that the agent puts an increased weight on avoiding 34 
losses instead of maximizing wins. Consequently, the agent explores the trusteeâ€™s behavior less, and 35 
starts avoiding any transactions with the trustee quicker compared to the healthy -to-optimistic agent, 36 
which may be a useful method to simulate social anhedonia (Barkus & Badcock, 2019). Several studies 37

suggest aberrant reward valuation and - responsiveness in depression (Alloy et al., 2016). On the other 2 
hand, perturbations in the ğ¶ parameter could be a proxy for rejection sensitivity (Gao et al., 2017) and 3 
risk aversion (Hartley & Phelps, 2012) , by placing an overly strong weight on avoiding the â€œlossâ€ 4 
outcome.  5 
Finally, we manipulated the agentâ€™s prior beliefs over contexts (ğ·, pessimistic priors). Particularly, the 6 
agentâ€™s generative model comprised the belief that a hostile context is more likely compared to a 7 
cooperative context a-priori. Individuals with depression expect to experience higher rates of  negative 8 
events, and lower rates of positive events (Thimm et al., 2013). Specifically, studies suggest that a lack 9 
of positive expectations may be more central to depression than the presence of negative expectations, 10 
which is in line with a reduced prior on positive experiences  (Horwitz et al., 2017) . In psychological 11 
terms, perturbations to the ğ· parameter may be related to interpersonal distrust (Poggi et al., 2019) or 12 
trauma (Mauritz et al., 2013) . In the simulation, a biased prior belief  was overridden by the agentâ€™s 13 
observations because the other parameters of the generative model (i.e., observation model, transition 14 
matrix, preference over outcomes) were intact. Pessimistic prior beliefs may effectively model 15 
experience-dependent negative beliefs that normalize in more favorable environments. Deviations in the 16 
ğ· vector may nevertheless be of relevance for clinical phenotypes, especially in combination with 17 
perturbations to other parameters of the generative model. 18
