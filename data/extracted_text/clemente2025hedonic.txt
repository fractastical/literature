Long abstract (â‰¤ 250 words) 
 
Hedonic evaluation shapes behaviour across organisms. Yet, the mechanisms that transform 
hedonic values into motivated behaviour remain poorly understood, and key questions remain 
unanswered. How do habits, wanting, and liking or disliking interact to generate behaviour? Do 
common principles govern behaviour across species and cognitive systems, from basic survival 
mechanisms to complex cultural endeavours? We propose a unifying theory of motivated 
behaviour that integrates hedonic evaluation with active inferenceâ€”a framework in which 
perception, action and cognition emerge from the imperative to minimise free energy. We argue 
that variational free energy provides a natural measure of liking and disliking, while expected 
free energy encodes wantingâ€”the motivational drive toward rewarding outcomes. This 
synthesis, which we term hedonic foraging, offers a principled explanation for how organisms 
navigate their environments based on pleasure-driven learning and decision-making. It posits 
that motivated behaviour emerges from the fundamental pursuit of maximising reward, with 
hedonic evaluation motivating and monitoring actions through wanting and liking mechanisms. 
We demonstrate that hedonic foraging captures a wide range of behaviours, from allostatic 
processes common to all animals to sophisticated human activities such as art appreciation and 
cultural exploration. Crucially, our integrative perspective fosters mutual enrichment between 
hedonic evaluation and active inferenceâ€”two fundamental approaches to behaviour. Ultimately, 
hedonic foraging reinforces the view that cognition is essentially about purposive engagement in 
adaptive interactions with the environment and its affordances and offers a unified framework 
for investigating cognition and behaviour.

1. Introduction 
Motivated behaviour is usually examined through highly specialised lenses, focusing on 
idiosyncratic biological adaptations, neural processes and cognitive mechanisms. This leads to 
a proliferation of theories specific to particular fields or taxa, with limited prospects for 
unification. Yet, one of the main endeavours of cognitive psychology and neuroscience is to 
unveil general mechanisms of behaviour (Berridge, 2004; Friston, 2010), raising the 
fundamental question: Is there a common mechanism driving behaviour across the spectrum of 
complexity and species? For instance, could the same principles explain a C. elegans orienting 
in a nutrient gradient, an endotherm foraging to maintain metabolic balance and a human 
formulating complex plans to achieve distant goals? 
 
When searching for unifying theories in biology and the behavioural sciences, evolution always 
emerges as a key principle (Gintis, 2007; Mesoudi et al., 2006). To be biologically meaningful, 
behaviour must be adaptive, enhancing the organism s chances of survival and reproduction by 
improving its interaction with the environment. Behavioural mechanisms are shaped by the need 
to ensure ontogenetic and phylogenetic success (Duckworth, 2009; Snell-Rood, 2013). 
 
Adaptive behaviour relies on evaluating the environmentâ€”its elements, relationships and 
affordances (Chiel & Beer, 1997; Schwarz, 2007). The fundamental neurobiological process of 
assessing stimuli as beneficial or harmful, rewarding or punishing, is referred to as hedonic 
evaluation. This process underpins the capacity to compare alternatives, make decisions and 
prioritise actions (Berridge & Kringelbach, 2015; Rosenthal, 2017). Whether selecting food, 
choosing a mate or engaging in leisure activities, organisms with a reward system are driven by 
the pursuit of rewards and avoidance of threats (Berridge & Kringelbach, 2008; Friston, 2013; 
Kringelbach, 2005; Rangel et al., 2008). This reward-seeking behaviour has been fundamental 
to brain evolution (Schultz, 2015). Since the emergence of chordates (Cisek, 2022), neurons in 
the reward system compute value signals by integrating sensory input with interoceptive and 
executive information, reflecting the organism's past experiences, states, goals and 
expectations (Rangel et al., 2008), thereby motivating and evaluating behaviour (Berridge & 
Kringelbach, 2013, 2015). 
 
Motivated behaviour unfolds in three interrelated phases, each involving specific mechanisms 
and value signals (Berridge & Kringelbach, 2013; Pessiglione & Lebreton, 2015). In the 
appetitive phase, wanting mechanisms anticipate the reward of behavioural outcomes, guiding 
approach or avoidance, biasing perception towards salient features and modulating neural 
activity in perceptual and cognitive systems. During the consummatory phase, these 
motivational aspects integrate with executive and motor systems to implement behavioural 
choices. Finally, in the satiety phase, liking (and disliking) mechanisms evaluate the behavioural 
outcomes, signalling reward and informing future behaviour through learning (Dayan, 2022).1 
Hedonic values are thus affective states that reward or punish behavioural decisions and inform 
future behaviour (Berridge & Kringelbach, 2015).

behaviour are not fully understood. For example, how does the pleasurable taste of a fruit urge

This limitation arises because hedonic evaluation lacks a computational mechanism for

relationships between hedonic value and actual behaviour. 
 
The most primary behaviours are rooted in the biological imperative to maintain homeostasis, 
driven by evaluative processes that compare the organismâ€™s state with reference values 
(Cannon, 1929). This imperative provides a natural link to action, as deviations from 
homeostatic set points generate hedonic signals that motivate corrective behaviours to restore 
balance (Ramsay & Woods, 2014). For instance, organisms require precise glycaemia ranges, 
experiencing pleasure when approaching them (e.g., eating) and distress when deviating from 
them (e.g., feeling hungry or experiencing indigestion). Importantly, the behaviour to approach 
such preferences depends on the organism and the context of evaluation, as eating may be 
pleasurable when hungry but unpleasant when satiated (Berridge, 1991; Skov, 2023). 
Therefore, quantitative measures capturing the role of hedonic value in motivating behaviour, 
and thus a computational framework for motivated behaviour, must be grounded in comparisons 
with preferences and tailored to embodied, situated behaviour (Roth & Jornet, 2013; Shapiro, 
2011). 
 
The imperative of maintaining homeostasis has long been recognised as central for living 
organisms, with the view that deviations trigger corrective mechanisms through reflexive actions 
(Cannon, 1929). However, recent approaches focus on the anticipatory nature of physiological 
regulation, a process called allostasis (Kleckner et al., 2017; Sterling, 2012). Allostasis implies a 
crucial shift from a reactive to a predictive perspective on the brain, because a predictive brain 
anticipates future events, objects and demands (Clark, 2013). This has two key implications: 
First, the brain projects its preferred future outcomes, generating internal representations of the 
consequences of possible actions (Conant & Ross Ashby, 1970; Pezzulo & Cisek, 2016). 
Second, the brain commands anticipatory actions, namely those that, according to its internal 
model, lead to its preferred outcomes (Wiener, 1948).  
 
These two implications call for an enactive approach whereby the brain does not merely store 
internal models of the world to infer the causes of incoming information but, more 
fundamentally, develops models that enable a dynamic interaction between the organism and 
its environment. Such an interaction entails estimating conditions and affordances upon which it 
may act to fulfil its needs and goals. This perspective brings together two pivotal heritages in 
conceptualising and understanding perception, and the very raison dâ€™Ãªtre of perception itself 
(Friston, 2010; Pezzulo et al., 2024). First, in Helmholtzâ€™s view (von Helmholtz, 1866), 
perception is a process of inference of the causes of the incoming sensory input. The brain 
constantly combines top-down information from an internal generative model and bottom-up 
sensory evidence in order to estimate what is going on in the environment. This view has been 
framed using a proper probabilistic setting in the Bayesian brain hypothesis (Doya et al., 2007; 
Gregory, 1980; Pouget et al., 2013; Rao & Ballard, 1999). Second, in Gibsonâ€™s (Gibson, 1986) 
ecological theory of perception, the brainâ€™s primary function is to perceive affordances, i.e., 
chances for action in the environment. In this action-oriented perspective, the brain is directly

To meaningfully explain and predict motivated behaviour, a mechanistic framework must 
therefore integrate predictive and enactive perspectives on the brain, proposing a probabilistic 
brain inference account of affordances in Bayes-optimal behaviour (Friston et al., 2006; Kording 
& Wolpert, 2006). Moreover, it must adopt an evolutionary perspective grounded in the simple 
axiom that an organismâ€™s behaviour serves the imperative to survive in its environment (Friston, 
2010). This axiom implies that organisms maintain life by adopting adaptive behaviours that 
bring them to preferred states, reflecting optimal living conditions within their ecological niche 
(Friston et al., 2012; Pezzulo et al., 2015). 
 
Active inference provides a mathematical framework to operationalise such a process (Friston, 
2010). Specifically, in active inference, behaviour reflects the imperative to minimise free 
energy, a proxy for the divergence between actual and preferred states (Friston, 2010; Pezzulo 
et al., 2024). In this article, we demonstrate how a broad range of behaviours can be accounted 
for by integrating hedonic evaluation with the core constructs of active inference, thus providing 
a biologically plausible computational framework for investigating behaviour. We demonstrate 
the potential of the proposed unified framework by modelling behaviour along a continuum, from 
basic allostatic processes shared by all organisms to more complex activities typically deemed 
uniquely human, such as art appreciation. This proposal allows us to bridge the gap and clarify 
the causal relationship between fundamental physiological processes involved in hedonic 
evaluation and action selection, offering a cohesive explanation for the motivational 
underpinnings of behaviour across different levels of complexity. By framing hedonic evaluation 
within active inference, we can better understand how organisms, from simple life forms to 
humans, navigate their environments, make decisions and pursue goals in a way that balances 
immediate rewards with long-term survival and well-being. 
 
We start by deriving a correspondence between the main components of hedonic evaluation, 
namely hedonic impact (core liking or disliking) and incentive salience (core wanting) (Berridge 
& Robinson, 1998), and the three components that guide policy choice in active inference, 
namely free energy, expected free energy and habit (K. Friston et al., 2017). From this 
correspondence, we propose a mechanism by which hedonic evaluation drives behaviour: 
hedonic foraging. Following this exposition, we showcase hedonic foraging with a series of 
example scenarios to illustrate its suitability to account for a continuum of behaviours. We argue 
that hedonic foraging ascribes complex cognitive functions such as appreciating art to the set of 
behaviours already accounted for by active inference, therefore welding core biological 
mechanisms and sophisticated cultural pursuits. We next discuss hedonic foraging in relation to 
existing proposals and the implications and contributions of the framework. To conclude, we 
emphasise the consequences and potential of a unifying, enactive and embodied computational 
mechanistic framework for understanding cognition and behaviour.

2. Hedonic foraging: Hedonic evaluation as 
active inference 
As previously outlined, the processes by which hedonic evaluation, a core motivational process, 
translates into a framework for understanding and predicting behaviour remain undefined. In 
particular, there is no clear mechanism for computing distinct hedonic values, integrating them 
and guiding behaviour over time. To fill this fundamental gap and understand motivated 
behaviour, we propose mapping hedonic evaluation onto the constructs of active inference, a 
principled theory that explains and predicts embodied cognition and behaviour (Friston, 2010; 
Pezzulo et al., 2024). The central move is to express the role of hedonic evaluation in 
monitoring and motivating free energy minimisation. 
 
Free energy minimisation is at the core of active inference. The theory stems from the truism 
that organisms must maintain their bodily parameters within some limits to stay alive. This 
pursuit is framed using the concept of surprise2 (Friston, 2010; Pezzulo et al., 2024). Surprising 
observations (e.g., exteroceptive, interoceptive, proprioceptive) are those that deviate from 
reference observations, which are called preferences and reflect the range of values that are 
characteristic and beneficial for an organismâ€™s functioning (Friston, 2010). In active inference, 
preferences are modelled as expectations about observations reflecting states of the world 
aligned with the organismâ€™s needs and goals. Surprise thus corresponds to prediction errors 
when such expectations are not met. Active inference posits that a single, unified principle 
drives behaviour: the imperative of minimising surprise, or, more precisely, minimising a 
tractable proxy of surprise called free energy (Friston, 2010; Pezzulo et al., 2024) (Box 1).  
 
An organism minimises free energy through a process of inference based on a generative 
model, namely a probabilistic mapping between hidden states of the world ğ‘  and their 
associated observations ğ‘œ. The (true) states of the world are called hidden because the 
organism may not have direct access to the true unfolding processâ€”the generative processâ€”
occurring in the world (Box 1). Instead, the organism relies on observations to infer the 
underlying states. Following the tradition of perception as inferenceâ€”originating with Helmholtz 
and further developed in the Bayesian brain hypothesis (Doya et al., 2007; Gregory, 1980; Rao 
& Ballard, 1999; von Helmholtz, 1866)â€”, the inference process is conceived as an inversion of 
the generative model, estimating the most likely states of the environment given observations.  
 
Active inference extends Bayesian theories by incorporating action into the inference process. 
In this framework, the brain does not merely infer causes passively; it also generates actions to 
minimise discrepancies between predicted and actual observations. To minimise surprise and 
maintain their preferred states, organisms continuously invert their generative model, comparing 
predicted observations with actual observations, to actively reduce the gap between expected 
and observed outcomes. This surprise-minimisation process drives both perceptionâ€”updating 
beliefs as in the tradition of perception as inference (von Helmholtz, 1866)â€”and actionâ€”
modifying the environment to achieve preferred outcomes (Friston, 2010; Gallagher, 2017). 
Consequently, active inference is inherently enactive, incorporating action into the inference 
process to enable adaptive behaviour in a dynamic world (Friston, 2010; Pezzulo et al., 2024; 
Ramstead et al., 2020). For example, organisms cannot survive outside a specific range of 
glycaemic levels, and deviation with respect to their preferred (physiologically ideal) range

While active inference proposes a computational mechanism by which behaviour emerges from 
the drive to reach preferred states, hedonic evaluation provides organisms with information 
about the expected (wanting) and resulting (liking) values of behaviours to guide future actions 
(Dayan, 2022). Both the will and action of approaching preferences are positively signalled (e.g., 
the desire and pleasure of eating) whereas departing from preferences is negatively signalled 
(e.g., the displeasure of being hungry). In this proposal, we integrate hedonic evaluation and 
active inference to derive a general theory of how hedonic value motivates behaviour, and 
illustrate how the proposed framework, hedonic foraging, enables predicting behaviour. In the 
remainder of this section, we establish correspondences between the main components of 
hedonic evaluation and active inference, which form the foundation of our proposal, ultimately 
leading to hedonic foraging as a unified framework for understanding behaviour. 
2.1. Hedonic impact (core liking) as variational free energy 
From an active inference perspective, organisms seek states that best match their preferences 
(Friston, 2010). When an organism is closer to its preferred (expected) states, it experiences 
less surprise and hence reduced variational free energy (VFE). From the perspective of hedonic 
evaluation, these states are rewarding (Berridge & Kringelbach, 2013; Pessiglione & Lebreton, 
2015).  
 
We propose that VFE can thus be considered as a quantitative measure of hedonic impact or 
core liking (Box 1). This step constitutes our proposalâ€™s first and central component to build a 
framework that casts hedonic evaluation onto active inference. 
 
As an illustration, consider a predator that expects to be in the adaptive state of satiation. When 
the predator is hungry, this discrepancy leads to prediction errors in its brain. This mismatch 
between the actual (hunger) and desired observation (satiety) is associated with high free 
energy. As the animal eats, this mismatch decreases, reducing VFE and eliciting a rewarding 
sensation. Similarly, when a person listens to Western classical music, they may experience 
pleasure when a perfect cadence resolves to the tonic. This fulfilment of expectations after the 
uncertainty introduced by the dominant chord corresponds to a state of lower VFE and a 
pleasurable listening experience (Cheung et al., 2019; Gold et al., 2019; Koelsch, Vuust, et al., 
2019). In both examples, VFE provides a measure for the hedonic impact (liking) of the chosen 
behaviour. Such a quantitative reference is crucial to compare and motivate behaviour.

2.2. Incentive salience (core wanting) as expected free energy 
While a reference measure for hedonic impact is necessary to understand motivated behaviour, 
it is not sufficient to predict it and thus explain how hedonic evaluation motivates behaviour. The 
reason is simple: VFE is retrospective; it evaluates incoming observations a posteriori and thus 
refers to past and present states. However, for planning and decision-making, an organism 
needs to infer the most effective future action or sequence of actions (hereafter referred to as 
policies) that generate observations yielding the most positive hedonic value, which are 
signalled by the lowest VFE. This cannot be done by simply considering past and current 
observations because actions must be chosen to bring about preferred observations in the 
future. To infer optimal policies, the organism must predict sequences of future states and 
observations and, for each possible policy, estimate its resulting VFE, that is, compute its 
expected free energy (EFE) (Box 1). The prospective nature of EFE enables organisms to 
evaluate and select policies based on their impact on future states. EFE extends VFE to 
account for planning, decision-making, exploration and curiosity, making it central to active 
inference as a theory of goal-directed behaviour (Friston et al., 2015; K. J. Friston et al., 2017; 
Schwartenbeck et al., 2019). 
 
We propose EFE as a quantitative measure of incentive salience or core wanting. This 
constitutes the second component of our proposal (Box 1). EFE consists of two key elements: 
pragmatic value and epistemic value (Box 1, Eq 2).  
 
Minimising the pragmatic term of EFE (Eq 2 in Box 2) maximises the pragmatic value, that is, 
the expected utility or reward associated with a future state consequence of the organismâ€™s 
policy. This aspect of EFE evaluates future outcomes (i.e., observations in the future resulting 
from policies) according to the extent to which they would fulfil the organismâ€™s goalâ€”i.e., how 
close they would bring the organism to its preference. By maximising pragmatic value, 
organisms avoid adverse outcomes and maximise immediate reward, therefore driving 
exploitative behaviour (K. Friston et al., 2017; Schwartenbeck et al., 2019). For example, the 
anticipation of eating a prey enhances the likelihood of hunting it for a predator. Similarly, the 
anticipation of enjoying live music motivates people to go to a concert. In other words, hedonic 
evaluation tells us that organisms want what they believe they will like, and active inference 
reveals the causal relationship underlying their choice of the policy expected to best achieve 
their desired outcome. 
 
Minimising the epistemic term of expected free energy (Eq 2 in Box 2) maximises epistemic 
value, which represents the expected information gain of each policy. This drives explorative 
behaviour by favouring policies that maximise knowledge about the world, resolving 
uncertainties about the environment and minimising prediction error. In our proposal, the 
epistemic component relates to incentive salience by driving the pursuit of information that 
reduces uncertainty about the environment, even in the absence of immediate reward 
(Schwartenbeck et al., 2019). For example, a predator can explore new territories to improve 
access to higher-quality or more abundant prey, just as humans might seek out new artists or 
venues to enhance future musical experiences. 
 
In summary, minimising EFE embodies incentive motivation by increasing the likelihood of 
choosing policies that lead to outcomes with more positive hedonic value, both in the short and 
long term. This minimisation can be naturally cast onto core wanting (Berridge & Dayan, 2021; 
Berridge & Kringelbach, 2015), capturing the dual motivational pull of securing rewarding 
outcomes and fostering learning.

2.3. Learned hedonic values as habits 
 
In active inference, learning occurs at multiple time scales (Friston et al., 2016). A particularly 
interesting form of learning emerges from repeated behaviour, which mayâ€”although does not 
necessarilyâ€”result from consistently selecting policies that have proven beneficial in the past 
(Thorndike, 1911). Habits emerge from a history of repeated behaviours, reflecting the role of 
past actions and their associated hedonic values in motivating future behaviour (Seger & 
Spiering, 2011) (Box 1).  
 
As such, habit constitutes an essential drive, capturing long-term associations between 
expectations and outcomes to complete the map of interrelations between hedonic values and 
the constructs of active inference. While EFE drives goal-directed behaviour, characterised by 
adaptive flexibility to environmental contingencies (Dayan, 2009), habit accounts for recurrent 
behaviour defined by past reinforcements but possibly unrelated to current hedonic values; 
being thus automatic, computationally efficient and inflexible (Dolan & Dayan, 2013). 
 
Habit formation provides adaptive advantages, as habits may maximise efficiency in policy 
selection for recurrent scenarios when there is little evidence from data, as they minimise 
energetic costs and risks when the available options are invariable and no further exploration is 
required or desired (Dayan, 2009; Dolan & Dayan, 2013). For example, the habit of visiting a 
particular location at a particular time of day or year when prey is abundant can be greatly 
beneficial for a predator as it does not have to spend energy and take risks to find it. Similarly, 
the habit of attending concerts at a known venue may increase the odds to enjoy a pleasurable 
musical experience, as the listener has learned that previous experiences have been 
consistently pleasurable. 
2.4. Motivated behaviour as hedonic foraging 
 
The central idea of this proposal is that the mechanisms of motivated behaviour can be 
characterised as an interaction between habit, EFE minimisation (wanting) and VFE 
minimisation (liking). First, policy selection depends on the combination of habits ( , reflecting a 
history of previous policy selections and their resulting hedonic impacts: liking or disliking) and 
expected free energy ( , corresponding to wanting for available policies). Then, the selected 
policy leads to new observations, whose VFE ( , corresponding to liking or disliking the 
resulting outcomes) is used to evaluate the effectiveness of the policy. In this way, the hedonic 
components of habit and wanting define the prior expectations over policies, while their 
combination with liking determines the posterior for the selected policy, which in turn will inform 
subsequent priors. We refer to Box 2 for a detailed formalisation of hedonic foraging. 
 
Hedonic foraging can thus be understood as navigating a free energy landscape (Figure 1), 
using the probabilities of selecting actions provided by habits and EFE as a compass, with 
continual adjustments informed by the VFE resulting from each policy. At each step, the 
organism chooses a policy that optimises, with some stochastic variability, a balance between 
habit and wanting. After each policy, the organism evaluates the VFE based on the resulting 
observations, which is reflected in a degree of liking or disliking. This hedonic evaluation then 
updates the active inference process, modulating the organismâ€™s habits and wanting and, more 
generally, refining the generative model that guides its actions. Behaviour thus unfolds 
iteratively, with each iteration consisting of these two interdependent stages (Figure 1).

Scenario 2: Statistical learning and preference formation 
 
Statistical learning is the process by which an organism implicitly learns regularities in sensory 
input, shaping future expectations (Turk-Browne, 2012). When an input aligns well with learned 
patterns, this match triggers a positive affective response, as the sensory-cognitive systems are 
particularly suited to processing familiar features efficiently (Bornstein & Dagostino, 1992; Reber 
et al., 1998). Statistical learning thus subserves mere exposure effects, which refer to a 
tendency to develop liking for stimuli as a function of familiarity (Zajonc, 1968). These effects 
are shared with other animals and have been extensively demonstrated in humans to influence 
the hedonic evaluation of a broad range of stimuli, from food (Birch & Marlin, 1982; Pliner, 1982) 
to music (Pereira et al., 2011) and artworks (Leder, 2003; Reber et al., 2004).  
 
This scenario exposes a simple link between statistical learning and preference formation. Here, 
an organism freely chooses among three equally preferred locations. Two contain stimulus A 
while the other contains a different but equally rewarding stimulus B. That is, the organism has 
no initial preference for either stimulus or location over the other. Without any incentive to favour 
a specific location, stimulus A is twice as likely to be encountered. According to the mere 
exposure effect, a greater exposure reinforces preference for stimulus A over B. As the 
organism progressively learns the frequency of each stimulus and where to encounter them, the 
pursuit of reward creates a feedback loop in which frequent visits to locations with the preferred 
stimulus strengthen the preference for that stimulus, further increasing the likelihood of 
returning. This behaviour ultimately leads to establishing a habit of visiting the locations typically 
containing the preferred stimulus.

4. Contextualising hedonic foraging 
 
In this section, we review theoretical frameworks and computational models that explore the 
relationships between hedonic evaluationâ€”along with the related concepts of emotional valence 
and affective valueâ€”and motivated behaviour. We categorise these approaches into those 
grounded in active inference and those based on alternative theoretical paradigms. For each 
approach, we highlight its core principles, summarise its key findings and evaluate how it relates 
to hedonic foraging. 
 
4.1. Emotion, affect and epistemic curiosity 
 
Theories and models built on active inference that aim to establish principled links between 
hedonic value and behaviour have primarily focused on the subjective emotional impact of 
experiencesâ€”specifically, on emotional valence, the positive or negative quality of emotion. 
Joffily and Coricelli (Joffily & Coricelli, 2013) proposed an association of emotional valence with 
the rate of change of VFE. As in hedonic foraging, their framework introduces a meta-
construction in that it applies to any active inference process independently of the underlying 
specific generative model. In their proposal, emotional valence emerges from the dynamics of 
VFE, tracked through its first and second derivatives. These dynamics are directly tied to the 
organismâ€™s adaptation to its environment. In contrast to hedonic foraging, the association 
between emotional valence and free energy in Joffily and Coricelliâ€™s proposal was not extended 
to action, limiting its ability to explain or predict motivated behaviour. The reason is that this 
framework predated the development of EFE in active inference, which provides a formal 
mechanism to link affective states with action selection (Friston et al., 2015). 
 
Emotional valence and adaptive behaviour have also been connected through active inference 
via the theory of constructed emotion, which posits that emotions emerge as holistic, context-
dependent brain-body phenomena (Barrett, 2017). This theory highlights the role of predictive 
processes in constructing emotional experiences, with the brain dynamically modelling internal 
and external states to regulate the body (allostasis) and interpret sensory inputs. As part of this 
process, emotional valence reflects the brain s predictions about bodily and environmental 
changes. Hesp and colleagues (Hesp et al., 2021) extended this framework by introducing 
affective charge, a computational perspective that incorporates meta-cognition, explicitly linking 
confidence in action selection to affective valence and subjective fitness through second-order 
beliefsâ€”i.e., beliefs about beliefs. Affective charge can therefore be understood as a (signed) 
reward prediction error, where positive prediction errors increase the agentâ€™s confidence in its 
action model and vice versa (Hesp et al., 2021). Hedonic foraging, by contrast, emphasises the 
direct role of habit, wanting and liking in shaping behaviour. In sum, affective charge highlights 
the intrinsic link between affect, implicit metacognition and action, where biological systems infer 
and regulate their own affective states, while hedonic foraging focuses on the primary forces 
driving behavioural choices. The idea of affective charge can be integrated into the hedonic 
foraging framework as second-order monitoring of reward maximisation, i.e., of free energy 
minimisation.

the broader framework of active inference. Predictive coding suggests that the brain seeks to 
make sense of sensory input by inferring the hidden states of the environment through Bayesian

by the epistemic component of curiosity, which reflects expected information gain and the 
expectation of resolving uncertainty. They describe this process as an epistemic arc comprising 
three interconnected stages: curiosity, epistemic action and the resulting â€œAhaâ€ experiences, 
which they interpret as the foundation of aesthetic experiences. While their model draws on the 
process theory of active inference and acknowledges potential applications beyond art-related 
experiencesâ€”such as everyday sense-making processesâ€”it remains largely conceptual and 
lacks a quantitative framework. Our approach addresses this gap by providing a quantitative 
method for characterising epistemic arcs, situating them within a continuum of experiences not 
restricted to specific domains or species. Our formulation builds on active inference by offering a 
precise characterisation of the epistemic and pragmatic motivations that drive engagement with 
the world. By extending these principles, our approach underscores the importance of formal 
models in capturing the general mechanisms underlying curiosity-driven behaviours across 
diverse contexts, and how hedonic evaluation monitors and drives such behaviours. 
 
4.2. Other models of hedonic value 
 
Several approaches unrelated to active inference have proposed links between the processes 
involved in hedonic evaluation and motivated behaviour. Skov (Skov, 2023) offered a 
conceptual model explaining how nervous systems assign hedonic value to sensory objects, 
leading to action. As in the current proposal, hedonic values are inherently adaptive, shaped by 
the organismâ€™s survival needs and ever-changing environment. Liking and disliking trigger 
response patterns that enable organisms to recognise and react to situations that could aid or 
harm their survival. Therefore, sensory valuation is not a simple reflection of an objectâ€™s intrinsic 
properties but depends on the organismâ€™s idiosyncratic and current needs and evaluative 
context. For example, eating may elicit a positive hedonic value when the organism is in 
hypoglycaemia (as in Scenario 1 in Section 3) and a negative value when it is in 
hyperglycaemia (LÃ³pez-Gambero et al., 2019; Roh et al., 2016). While Skov s model remains 
conceptual and does not accommodate data or make predictions, its conceptual similarities with 
our proposal and its detailed description of the neurobiology of hedonic evaluation make it a 
valuable resource for refining and enhancing descriptions based on active inference. 
 
Our proposal shares several features with computational models of hedonic value based on 
reinforcement learning. Both reinforcement learning and active inference are theories of 
decision-making. In reinforcement learning, the objective is to determine the optimal policyâ€”the 
one that maximises rewardâ€”through a value function following Bellman s equation (Sutton & 
Barto, 2018). This function includes components for immediate rewards and expected future 
rewards. In a proposal focused on hedonic value (referred to as aesthetic value), Brielmann and

5. Why should we expect a continuum?  
 
Hedonic foraging integrates hedonic evaluation into a single, unified model of action and 
perceptionâ€”active inferenceâ€”to describe a comprehensive range of behaviours that 
transcends biological taxa. In this framework, the core constructs of active inference remain 
consistent, with only the modulating parameters being scenario-specific and tailored to each 
organism and behaviour. This is how continuum should be understood here: a single framework 
accounts for a range of observed phenomena, illustrating continuity across behavioural 
complexity and species endowed with an evaluation system. 
 
But why should we expect a continuum in the first place? This enquiry is central to many 
disciplines, as it carries fundamental scientific and philosophical implications for understanding 
cognition and human nature. In particular, it addresses whether there is a computational or 
neurophysiological mechanism of behaviour unique to Homo sapiens. If such a special 
mechanism exists, what does the break in the continuum look like, mean and entail for 
cognition? If a common mechanism drives a continuum of behaviours across scenarios and 
taxa, even sophisticated human pursuits and experiences traditionally deemed distinctly human 
(e.g., the so-called aesthetic experiences) would obey the same principles as allostatic 
processes in simpler organisms such as worms. We can refine the question to resolve this 
conundrum as follows: Should we expect wanting or liking nutritionally valuable food (a primary 
reward satisfying biological needs) and wanting or liking in the conscious appreciation of an 
artwork (a secondary reward without direct biological relevance) to share the same 
computational and neurophysiological mechanisms? 
 
Scholars in empirical aesthetics and related disciplines have traditionally insisted on a negative 
answer to this question, based on the widespread but controversial belief that art appreciation is 
disconnected from other psychological and neurological processes, as critically reviewed by 
Skov and Nadal (Nadal & Skov, 2024; Skov, 2019; Skov & Nadal, 2018, 2020). However, a 
central argument for a broadly shared basis of hedonic evaluation in motivated behaviour rests 
on substantial evidence that not only a wide array of pleasurable experiencesâ€”e.g., eating 
chocolate, having sex, listening to musicâ€”engages the same neural circuits in humans, but also 
that these circuits are evolutionarily conserved and widely shared with other animals (Berridge & 
Dayan, 2021; Berridge & Kringelbach, 2013, 2015). This supports the hypothesis of a common 
currency for hedonic evaluation (Levy & Glimcher, 2012; Montague & King-Casas, 2007), 
according to which first and secondary rewards are processed within a shared neural network 
across a range of species (Bartra et al., 2013; Sescousse et al., 2013).  
 
From an evolutionary perspective, the function of liking is not to generate positive introspective 
experiences but to enable organisms to identify and respond to adaptive opportunities and 
threats (Pessiglione & Lebreton, 2015; Rosenthal, 2017). Darwin (Darwin, 1872) anticipated this 
idea, proposing that emotional reactions are primarily selected for their adaptive value. This 
view has been substantiated by modern neuroscience, which provides extensive evidence that 
affective processing is underpinned by a core set of mechanisms shared across phylogeny 
(Anderson & Adolphs, 2014; LeDoux, 2012). Over time, cognitive and cultural pleasures have 
co-opted the hedonic circuitry initially evolved for basic sensory pleasures (Berridge & Dayan, 
2021; Nguyen et al., 2021). Core hedonic values (affective signals) appeared first, followed by 
subjective and conscious evaluations computed by more complex, hierarchical structures 
(Berridge & Kringelbach, 2015; Panksepp, 1998). It has been proposed that wanting evolved 
initially to pursue innate rewards, such as nutrients and mates, while liking likely emerged later

Crucially, hedonic foraging describes a common computational mechanism implemented 
through specific neurobiological processes. While the framework suggests that the principle of 
reward maximisation spans behaviours and species, the scenarios and the brain structures 
underlying hedonic evaluation differ between taxa. More complex brains enable more refined 
cognitive abilities and sophisticated computations, such as deeper policy evaluation and 
planning: for example, C. elegans search for food using simple exploration strategies (Calhoun

conserved across phylogeny, with biogenic amines (e.g., dopamine) playing a fundamental role 
in behavioural responses to rewards across various animal phyla (Barron et al., 2010). The 
emergence of the mesolimbic reward system predates the divergence of chordates (Yamamoto 
& Vernier, 2011) and there is substantial converging evidence for homology across major 
vertebrate lineages (O'Connell & Hofmann, 2011). Dopamine and other neurotransmitters 
mediate hedonic signals in sensory learning and motivate behaviour in invertebrates as well 
(Unoki et al., 2006). Moreover, neurogenetic studies suggest that the insect reward system has 
a modular structure that broadly parallels the mammalian system in terms of functional 
organisation, with distinct brain circuits for reward learning, wanting and liking (Perry & Barron, 
2013). 
 
Together, these functional, phylogenetic and neurophysiological similarities suggest a unified 
framework for understanding how hedonic evaluation guides behaviour across scenarios and 
taxa. As reviewed above, hedonic evaluation mechanisms evolved to motivate adaptive 
behaviour and are conserved across species (e.g., C. elegans, Homo sapiens), scenarios and 
stimuli (e.g., food, sex, artworks). Active inference explains this continuum from first principles, 
with behaviour driven by the imperative to maintain preferred states shaped by evolutionary 
adaptations to an ecological niche. By integrating hedonic evaluation within active inference, 
hedonic foraging provides the computational mechanism through which reward maximisation 
drives and monitors behaviour in organisms with a reward system, situating the commonalities 
discussed above within a continuum of behaviours and species.

6. Implications and potential of the hedonic foraging framework 
 
6.1. Contributions to the hedonic evaluation and active inference 
frameworks 
 
We have presented hedonic foraging as a computational mechanism through which reward 
maximisation drives behaviour in organisms with a reward system, with hedonic evaluation 
monitoring and motivating free energy minimisation relative to specific, embodied affordances 
and conditions. The theoretical and mathematical integration of hedonic evaluation and active 
inference entails a reciprocal enrichment of both frameworks, advancing our understanding of 
how motivated behaviour aligns with reward-driven behaviour. In the remainder of this section, 
we succinctly discuss the implications of merging hedonic evaluation and active inference in 
hedonic foraging, and how this integrated approach addresses the central questions raised in 
the introduction. 
 
Through hedonic foraging, active inference makes several key contributions to hedonic 
evaluation. Traditionally, hedonic evaluation has lacked a formal mechanism for quantifying and 
comparing hedonic values and translating these values into decisions and actions. In our 
proposal, active inference fills this gap by providing hedonic evaluation with a computational 
framework for understanding motivated behaviour. By formalising hedonic evaluation within the 
active inference framework, researchers can systematically examine how the interplay of factors 
such as internal states and environmental cues leads to behavioural outcomes. Crucially, 
hedonic foraging unifies wanting and liking, the core components of hedonic evaluation, as 
complementary facets of a single objective: maximising reward, that is, minimising VFE. 
Furthermore, this approach embraces the common currency hypothesis in that it acknowledges 
a single evaluative mechanism regardless of reward type, and transcends it by proposing a 
unified mechanism for behaviour across complexity and taxa grounded in free energy 
minimisation. In doing so, active inference provides hedonic evaluation with a first principle 
account of behaviour, linking neurobiological processes to computational goals. 
 
Conversely, through our framework, hedonic evaluation offers significant insights with the 
potential to enhance and refine active inference. The mechanisms underlying evaluation and 
reward are increasingly well defined in active inference. Reward maximisation is integrated 
through prior preferences that encode desired outcomes. These preferences guide policy 
selection by minimising EFE, which balances exploration and exploitation. Additionally, active 
inference formalises reward-related behaviour by treating prediction errors and learning rates as 
precision-weighted processes, akin to mechanisms in reinforcement learning but grounded in a 
unifying Bayesian framework. Crucially, dopamine has emerged as a key candidate for 
encoding precision-weighted prediction errors in active inference. Phasic dopamine signals are 
thought to represent transient changes in precision, guiding learning and action by signalling 
reward prediction errors (Bogacz, 2020; Friston et al., 2012), while tonic dopamine signals 
(Bogacz, 2020), or the locus coeruleusâ€“noradrenaline system (Sales et al., 2019), modulate the 
baseline confidence in policies, balancing exploratory and exploitative behaviour. 
 
Despite these neurobiological formulations of active inference, much remains unknown about its 
precise neurobiological correlates. In particular, the neurobiological implementation of VFE 
minimisation is not so well formalised in active inference, and that of the correspondence 
between habits EFE and VFE has not yet been clarified. Thanks to its grounding in hedonic 
evaluation, the hedonic foraging framework outlines possible directions to address these gaps.

6.2. Explaining and predicting empirical data 
 
The value of any theory or model resides in its heuristic utility and predictive powerâ€”its ability to 
explain existing phenomena and anticipate new data (Lipton, 2005). The central concept for 
understanding how hedonic foraging can address empirical questions is computational 
phenotyping. At the core of hedonic foraging, regardless of the scenario under scrutiny, lies a 
generative model, which establishes a mapping between model parameters and behaviour. 
Computational phenotyping is the process of retrieving model parameters from empirical data 
by inverting such mapping (Schwartenbeck & Friston, 2016). Computational phenotyping has 
emerged as a powerful tool for characterising individual variability in domains such as social 
behaviour (Xiang et al., 2012), personality, developmental and clinical neuroscience (Patzelt et 
al., 2018), cognition (Schurr et al., 2024) and genetics (Drouin et al., 2016). It has also been 
applied to active inference, particularly in computational psychiatry (Schwartenbeck & Friston, 
2016) and clinical neuroscience (Smith et al., 2021), offering a dual benefit: First, it harnesses 
the power of active inferenceâ€”with its principled formulation and parameters interpretable in 
terms of cognitive processesâ€”to provide a deeper understanding of cognitive phenomena. 
Second, it enables the simulation and prediction of behaviour based on empirically derived 
parameters.  
 
Hedonic foraging promotes the application of computational phenotyping to empirical

precedence (Barnett et al., 2007). Analysing behaviour in the context of predationâ€”and other

7. Conclusion 
 
In this paper, we propose a natural operationalisation of hedonic values in behaviour by 
mapping the phases of hedonic evaluation and their corresponding value signals onto the core 
components of active inference. In our interpretation, VFE corresponds to hedonic impact or 
core liking, quantifying sensory pleasureâ€”or displeasureâ€”of ongoing experiences, while EFE 
captures the motivational drive or core wanting to pursue rewarding outcomes. This synthesis 
forms the foundation of our theoretical framework, hedonic foraging, which describes how 
hedonic evaluation generates and evaluates motivated behaviour. 
Grounded in active inference, hedonic foraging provides a unifying framework for understanding 
preference-seeking behaviours through principles akin to those governing physical systems. 
Drawing on analogies from statistical physics, it conceptualises organisms as agents navigating 
probabilistic spaces to minimise VFEâ€”similar to particles minimising a functional such as the 
Lagrangian (Friston, 2013; Friston et al., 2023). In this view, hedonic evaluation guides 
organisms along behavioural trajectories that minimise surprise, mirroring the principle of least 
action in physics. Crucially, this predictive framework relies on an embodied model of the 
worldâ€”shaped by an organism s ecological nicheâ€”that generates expectations about and 
evaluates the outcomes of actions in the form of hedonic values. 
A key strength of hedonic foraging is its ability to unify diverse preference-seeking behaviours 
under a shared mechanistic framework. This extends the common currency hypothesis beyond 
static neural correlates of hedonic value to highlight the dynamic mechanisms underpinning 
hedonic choice in action planning. In doing so, our proposal bridges fundamental biological 
processes with higher-order cultural endeavours typically considered uniquely human, 
suggesting shared roots in adaptive mechanisms. 
Ultimately, hedonic foraging reinforces the idea that cognition is not primarily about abstract 
reasoning or knowledge accumulation but about engaging in adaptive interactions with the 
environment and its affordances. This perspective aligns with the growing recognition that 
purposive interaction is key to understanding cognition (Pezzulo et al., 2024) and represents a 
promising avenue for advancing artificial intelligence. The move from traditional milestones in 
the field, such as the Turing test to the embodied Turing test (Zador et al., 2023), reflects this 
paradigm shift. We hope our proposal will inspire further investigation into the neurobiology of 
motivated behaviour, bridging foundational biological mechanisms with complex cognitive 
functions and fostering interdisciplinary scientific progress.
