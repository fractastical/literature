Abstract 
We combine theoretical biology and systems neuroscience to relate the mechanisms of post-
traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) models to biopsychosocial complexity. Moving beyond fear 
conditioning, extinction and habits formed by associative learning, we consid er conserved 
neuromodulatory mechanisms related to harm expectation, defensive mobilisation, and 
context (in)sensitivity. Our account of harm-mitigating, neurobehavioural responses furnishes 
a mechanistic understanding of maladaptive behaviour and impaired  belief-updating, which 
are often underspecified in conceptual analyses. Grounded in neurobiological evidence , we 
specify PTSD -related impairment in terms of Bayesian belief -updating, and establish an 
adaptive/maladaptive boundary using active inference and simulation studies . The ensuing 
model plausibly illustrates PTSD pathogenesis, which remains understudied relative to 
aetiology. We locate pathogenesis in maladaptive precision following traumatic learning, 
characterised by aberrant Bayesian credit assignment. Future scient ific and clinical studies 
can test the validity of these insights, which could underwrite our understanding of the 
phenomenology, diagnostics and ultimately treatment intervention in post-traumatic disorders.

During traumatic experiences, the imperative to mitigate against severe harm requires an 
intense mobilisation of biological subsystems, an ‚Äòemergency response mode‚Äô. 1,2 However,  
multiple factors involved in the traumatic experience, its traumatic memory and the 
experience-dependent plasticity of the traumatised person can lead this emergency response 
mode to become persistent, that is, maladaptive. Here, we focus on modelling post-traumatic 
pathogenesis as the process by which a trauma schema (or ‚Äòmode‚Äô) becomes persistent and, 
in turn, causes a wide range of post -traumatic stress syndromes (PTSS), including those 
related to mental imagery (e.g., ‚Äòflashbacks‚Äô). The modelling also illustrates the process of 
returning to a pre -traumatic response mode, to sugg est how PTSS treatment interventions 
could target the root cause of symptoms. In contrast to reductionist accounts that can lead to 
over-medicalisation, this computational approach is consistent with a biopsychosocial 
understanding of post-traumatic disorders. 
 
The present work models conserved neuromodulatory and sensorimotor mechanisms related 
to harm expectation, defensive mobilisation, 3,4 and the associative learning of causes and 
consequences (model -based learning). 5,6 The modelling remains compatible with multiple 
accounts of fear and extinction,7‚Äì10 without relying on these constructs, by shifting the focus to 
the general construct of harm. Perceptibly harmful administrations of pain, electric shock, 
restraint, etc., are common to experimental paradigms of fear conditioning and extinction in 
(non-human) animals. A capacity for harm perception, expectation, and mitigation is 
necessarily evolutionarily conserved, as an abundance of harm negatively impacts the survival 
of individuals and species. 
 
The notion of expectation has been readily operationalised using a range of comparable 
theories including Hebbian learning, spike -timing dependent plasticity (STDP), predictive 
coding, and embodied cognition frameworks broadly known under ‚ÄòBayesian brain‚Äô or 
‚Äòpredictive processing‚Äô descriptions, sometimes specified as active inference (applied here). 
These theoretical approaches relate to methodologies for understanding and measuring 
correspondences between neural substrates and behaviour. Computational psy chiatry 
commonly incorporates these methodologies, 11 found in an emerging body of research on 
understanding traumatic experience and PTSS.1,12‚Äì14 
 
Neurobehavioural response patterns necessary for survival form the basis of our PTSD 
modelling. The sequence of agent -environment interactions engaging these neural 
mechanisms are: (1) learning about the sources of harm in multiple contexts; (2) learning when 
harm is probable; (3) making a (fallible) judgement about the likelihood of imminent harm; and 
(4) responding defensively only when harm is likely. Grounded in the anticipation of harm 
under varied conditions, the model remains consistent with experime ntal studies of fear 
conditioning and extinction in animal models,9 in addition to observational and case studies of 
human PTSD and related clinical trials. 15 Nevertheless, we argue that probabilistic learning 
about contexts is central to understanding post-traumatic impairments,5,16 beyond associative 
(model-free) mechanisms often used to describe traumatic learning. 
 
From this perspective, there are extensive commonalities between non -traumatic and 
traumatic experiences. By drawing on the sciences of visual attention, multimodal perception, 
memory, and sleep, and combining them with neuromodulatory systems neuroscience , we 
articulate a general neuropsychological and neurobehavioral account that illustrates a putative 
pathogenesis of disorders related to sequelae of traumatic stress including PTSD and CPTSD. 
This account informs a novel neurocomputational model that highlights biopsychosocial 
dynamics relevant to diagnosis and treatment of these disorders.

From the standpoint of an individual in a new situation, there may be ambiguity as to whether 
the surrounding context is one in which harm is likely (harmful context) or unlikely (harmless 
context). Ordinarily, determinations of context (harmful vs. harmle ss) are made by gathering 
sensory evidence to reach a decision. The belief that I am in one context and not the other 
should ideally persist to some extent as a (contextual) prior belief, but should also decay, in 
order to strike a balance between (a) maintaining the belief that I am still in the same context 
and (b) re-assessing whether the context has changed. 
 
Our theory describes PTSD pathogenesis as a process by which the balance between 
harmless and harmful contextual priors is lost. Here, the prior belief in a harmful context grows 
(in precision) beyond a critical tipping point. Once reached, the aforementioned decay rate is 
insufficient for rebalancing with contextual priors for harmless context. 
 
Following traumatic exposure to a harmful context, the imbalance occurs depending on 
resilience or vulnerability. For a resilient phenotype, the learning rate for harmful context is 
sufficient to preclude an imbalance between contextual priors. In contrast, if prior beliefs about 
the presence of a harmful context become unduly strong, then opportunities for re-assessing 
context will be continually foregone. The foregoing of contextual re-assessment will persist for 
harmless contexts, due to the (false) beli ef they are harmful. Moreover, without re -
assessment, a (false) conclusion that the context is harmful will in turn be (mis)counted as 
new (false) evidence that the context is indeed harmful ‚Äì thus continually strengthening the 
false contextual belief in a positive feedback loop, a ‚Äòvicious circle‚Äô. This miscounting can be 
described as part of a confirmation bias in perceptual decision -making modelled by 
hierarchical approximate inference.18 
 
If PTSD pathogenesis occurs, a possible treatment intervention relates to the provision of 
evidence that I am in a harmless context, rather than a harmful one as believed a priori. For 
the treatment to succeed, I must accumulate enough evidence to update my contextual priors. 
Only then can I seek out novel situations and determine their true context, rather than giving 
undue weight to the harmful prior. 
 
Below, we introduce a neuromodulatory circuit for a mechanistic understanding of PTSD -
related phenomena. We then illustrate PTSD pathogenesis and treatment in model -based 
simulation studies (see Results). To draw on the literature that supports our account, we also 
present an interlinked cognitive psychological framing of processes and mechanisms related 
to context recognition (see Online Methods). Under this framing, we identify context ual 
interactions with what is widely understood as Visual Working Memory (VWM). We also cover 
contextual interactions between long -term memory (LTM) and task -related generalisation. 
Interested readers may wish to have this general cognitive psychological description in place 
first as a foundation. 
Neuromodulatory circuits and PTSD pathogenesis 
We first focus on putative functional neuroanatomy, to hypothesize the neural pathways that 
subserve the psychological, cognitive, and physiological processes involved in our account. 
As an initial step, we draw connections between PTSS and a pontine neuro modulatory 
subsystem. We then provide a schema that brings this subsystem into correspondence with 
non-pathological and pathological neurobehavioral dynamics and relate these to psychiatric 
diagnostic criteria. Finally, we elaborate a long -range circuit re levant to pathogenesis and 
treatment of PTSD.

The reticular activating system (RAS) in the brainstem, implicated in sleep -wake cycle 
regulation, is a neurobiological locus of a range of sleep disorders connected to psychiatric 
conditions.19‚Äì21 Under typical functioning, excitatory cholinergic projections from the 
pedunculopontine tegmental nucleus (PPtg) to the locus coeruleus (LC) stimulate an inhibitory 
noradrenergic projection from the LC to the PPtg. In this PPtg -LC (excitation -inhibition) 
interaction, acetylcholine (ACh) and noradrenaline (NA) are mutually stabilising, resulting in a 
balance characteristic of attentive wakefulness. At the initiation of sleep, the PPtg continues 
to emit ACh but temporarily ceases excitation of the LC, resulting in a marked rise in ACh and 
diminishment of NA. If, however, this pontine ACh/NA subsystem is at an (aberrant) elevated 
tonic level, one would expect sleep to involve vivid trauma replay nightmares (elevated ACh) 
and gross body movements (elevated NA), both of which are characteristic of PTSD.21‚Äì25 
 
The elevated tonic level may be held over from an adaptive (i.e., situationally apt) phasic 
elevation during a wakeful traumatic experience. During such an event, phasic elevation would 
aid harm mitigation efforts by increasing ACh for selective sensory gain and increasing NA for 
rapid behavioural response. Known PTSS are depicted in Fig. 1, in relation to ACh and NA 
levels. 
 
 
 
FIGURE 1. Across the central axis, the difference in relative ACh and NA levels in awake and asleep 
states is depicted (diagonal dotted lines). Green is used to indicate unimpaired tonic levels that raise 
to phasic levels during a traumatic experience and return t o unimpaired tonic levels (green) for a 
resilient individual (movement along the grey arrows). A vulnerable individual may remain at elevated 
phasic levels (red) such that they become tonic. The text boxes indicate corresponding consequences 
of ACh and NA when unelevated or elevated, including PTSD symptoms under impaired tonic 
elevation (awake and asleep). 
 
Following such an event, phasic levels should fall ahead of healthy sleep, such that memory 
can be re -encoded and re -organised in relation to event boundaries. Future reactions to 
trauma cues in new contexts could deploy attention to the sensory periphery to update beliefs 
about the veridical context (see Fig. 8, boxes A and 4A). However, if the phasic increase has 
not subsided prior to sleep, then disrupted sleep can hinder memory re -encoding and re -
organisation, resulting in stronger subsequent activation during traumatic recall. This overly 
strong reactivation can provide sufficient evidence for a false context such that belief-updating 
about veridical conditions is hindered by peripheral sensory attenuation (see Fig. 8, boxes B1, 
B2, and 4B). If the phasic elevation endures, it can become effectively a new elevated tonic 
baseline, with wakeful hypervigilance and hyperarousal. Fig. 2 illustrates how phasic elevation 
can become tonic elevation, and relates this to PTSD diagnostic criteria.

For a diagnosis of post -traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), the prototypical disorder of its kind, 
symptoms must last ‚Äúlonger than a month‚Äù according to the DSM -5 but ‚Äúat least several weeks‚Äù 
according to the ICD -11. From a computational point of view, the o ptimal decay rate should be 
determined by the persistence of high probability of harm. If, say, serious harm is likely to occur within 
three weeks, high alert should not stop within one week. Hence, we must not pathologize the initial 
occurrence of PTSD sy mptoms, but focus on how their nature increases the risk of unwarranted, 
maladaptive persistence. 
 
We postulate a critical relationship between a ‚Äòreturn slope‚Äô (B) and its intercepting a threshold 
separating extreme from ordinary arousal. This intercept characterises the transition out of PTSS  ‚Äì 
the time axis would be set differently in different diagnostic classifications and related to different 
neurobiological indicators (e.g., neurochemical and connectome properties 26). Resilience can then 
be related to the ability to clearly infer that one no longer faces an increased likelihood of harm, and 
rapidly return to the psychophysiological baseline  if so (ability to ‚Äòbounce back‚Äô) . A vulnerability to 
PTSS can be characterised with an inappropriately sluggish decay of arousal. Vulnerability factors 
may include genetic predispositions and lifespan events such as earlier psychological trauma or 
physical traumatic brain injury (TBI).

One ‚Äòupstream‚Äô reason the pontine ACh-NA subsystem may be disordered in wake and sleep 
is reduced activation of the serotonergic (5HT) inhibitory ‚Äòbrake‚Äô from the Dorsal Raphe 
Nucleus (DRN). The inhibition and disinhibition emerges through a complex interplay of GABA 
and glutamate (Glu.) from the prefrontal cortex (PFC), limbic systems (LS), and basal ganglia 
(BG).27 Notably, altered GABA and Glu. levels and ratios have been found in PTSD impairment 
in contrast to unimpaired controls. 28 Taken together, thes e findings suggest a long -range 
circuit, by which forebrain interactions across the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) can dampen 
DRN activity, thereby reducing the inhibitory influence of 5HT on pontine subsystems including 
the RAS, and especially the PPtg a nd LC. The PPtg and LC neuronal populations are the 
primary emitters of ACh and NA, respectively, with innervation throughout the brain in addition 
to reciprocal projections. 
 
Excessive strength of the initial encoding of a traumatic experience is suggested by the 
increase in cholinergic stimulation from the PPtg to the Ventral Tegmental Area (VTA) and 
Substantia Nigra (SN). This ACh stimulation in turn stimulates a dopaminergic (DA) pathway 
to the forebrain (PFC, LS, and other parts of the BG). A DA surge is known to increase memory 
plasticity and encoding strength, especially during emotionally significant experience.29‚Äì31 The 
full circuit is depicted in Fig. 3.

Increased serotonin (5HT) inhibits the pontine subsystem activity, thereby reducing its NA and ACh 
output. Conversely, decreased 5HT from the DRN disinhibits the pontine subsystem activity, allowing 
NA and ACh levels to rise. 
 
The DRN is excited or inhibited by the interaction between glutamate (Glu.) and gamma-aminobutyric 
acid (GABA), via glutamatergic and GABAergic projections from a richly interconnected network 
situated primarily in the forebrain. This forebrain network (with some outer nodes) is comprised of the 
prefrontal cortex (PFC), limbic system (LS), and the basal ganglia (BG), and also includes the anterior 
cingulate cortex (ACC) (unlabelled). When a harmless context is inferred via the forebrain network, 
the DRN ‚Äòbrake‚Äô is applied; its serotonergic projections increase the 5HT input to the pons, inhibiting 
NA and ACh levels. When a harmful context is inferred, the DRN is inhibited (the ‚Äòbrake‚Äô is released). 
In turn, the decreased 5HT input disinhibits the pontine sub system, leading NA and ACh levels to 
rise. 
 
PTSD pathogenesis can be understood in relation to the circuit diagram as follows. A current 
or recalled traumatic experience releases the DRN ‚Äòbrake‚Äô to raise ACh/NA for enhanced 
harm-mitigation (defensive response). A current harmful context will be strongly encoded with 
the potential to be easily cued. Later on, however, a resilient individual may experience cued 
traumatic recall, but with insufficient strength to resolve uncertainty about the present. This 
leads to peripheral sensory sampling, belief -updating about the veridical present, and 
subsequent applying of the DRN ‚Äòbrake‚Äô to lower ACh/NA (see Fig. 2). 
 
In a vulnerable individual, the overly strong recall of a harmful context may cascade through 
elevated ACh/NA, stimulated forebrain Glu. perfusion, and increased top-down excitement of 
VWM. Blending present visual sensation and recalled imagery in VWM (as described in Online 
Methods) can lead to false contextual inference with rapidly evinced behaviours that an agent 
(falsely) believes are contextually apt. Thus, instead of an exploratory policy to resolve 
uncertainty about context, an exploitation policy i s selected; namely, peripheral sensory 
attenuation and vigorous defence (e.g., fight or flight). This is consistent with a role for ACh 
enhancing attention to significant cues,32,33 amounting to hypervigilance (readily cuing harmful 
context), while NA simultaneously enhances motor responsivity,34 amounting to hyperarousal 
(see Fig. 1).  
Results 
Neurocomputational simulations 
We developed a neurocomputational model of interacting systems perspectives schematised 
here (see above and Online Methods): the neurocognition of trauma associations  including 
learning, memory, and context; the phenomenology and symptoms of PTSD and their relation 
to treatment intervention; and the neurobehavioural dynamics of and a neuromodulatory circuit 
for defensive mobilisation. We show that our neurocomputational implementation explains (i) 
natural waning of PTSS in resilient individuals under condit ions of ordinary stress, (ii) 
persistence of such symptoms in predisposed individuals in similar conditions, (iii) recovery of 
context disambiguation through a learning -based intervention, and (iv) the limited role of 
symptom reduction through monoaminergic therapy on its own. 
 
We simulated PTSD pathogenesis and treatment intervention based on a generative model 
that refines, extends, and operationalises previous work. 1,11,12 The simulation studies use a 
generative model (Fig. 4) to reproduce the pathogenesis of PTSD following a traumatic 
encounter and a successful and unsuccessful intervention. The learning and behavioural 
dynamics of the system are driven by uncertainty reso lution (implemented as free energy 
minimisation).

contextually ambiguous observation (triangle). During the initial traumatic experience, the 
system advances along the horizontal arrow from the initial state to the second state. The 
state sequence is controlled by an exploratory scene sampling policy (‚Äòtwo eyes‚Äô pictogram) 
to resolve uncertainty about the context. This second state generates adverse (surprising) 
observations that can include a range of ‚Äòharmful‚Äô sensations, among them pain (e.g., being 
struck by shrapnel) and visual scenes that inflict moral injury (e.g., bearing witness to extreme 
suffering). The observation allows for a veridical contextual inference, updating the prior 
probability (red D) that one is in the harmful context. 
 
Following the harmful traumatic experience, in a new harmless context, one begins in the initial 
state conditioned by the green D. This start state also generates a contextually ambiguous 
observation (triangle). In other words, the initial observation (triangle) is insufficient to 
disambiguate between the two alternative start states. The exploratory policy would progress 
the state sequence along the horizontal line to generate context -specific observations 
(squares). This disambiguates the two contexts, le ading to a veridical inference of the 
harmless context. 
 
However, if one begins in the ‚Äòsquares‚Äô initial state (conditioned by the green D), and the 
contextual prior of the past (traumatic) context is unduly precise, it is sufficient to resolve the 
uncertainty about the context, such that the exploratory policy is not selected (it is falsely 
believed to be uninformative due to overly informa tive prior beliefs). This results in false 
contextual inference and selection of the (peripheral) sensory attenuation policy (‚Äòpartly 
covered eyes‚Äô pictogram). We emphasise that this form of sensory attenuation is consistent 
with hypervigilance, amounting to rapid shifting of foreground focus without resolving 
contextual uncertainty.

The resulting landscapes at the conclusion of both the resilient and vulnerable processes are 
consistent with recent neural recordings of macaque perceptual decision -making.36 In the 
macaque study, neurons accumulate evidence for two alternative visual fixation target options 
preceding a saccade-to-target action. When there is ambiguity between the targets, decision 
time is longer, as sensory evidence accumulates to resolve the ambiguity. When there is bias 
towards one option, the decision time to reach a c ommitment (action) is faster, and the bias 
functions as greater ‚Äòpre-accumulated‚Äô, or notional, evidence. 
 
Our case is not about alternative perceptual targets, but the interpretation is similar. 37 
Following a cue (triangle) common to two contexts (squares and crosses), if there is 
insufficient evidence to resolve which context is present, then more evidence accumulation is 
required to resolve the ambiguity (higher decision time to commit). In our m odel, this is 
achieved by an additional timestep of peripheral sensory sampling, to accumulate contextual 
evidence for either squares or crosses (see Fig. 8, boxes 4A  and A). However, if there is a 
strong bias for one context (crosses), the contextual inference for crosses is made 
immediately, even if this amounts to committing to the incorrect decision when the true context 
is otherwise, i.e., false inference (see Fig. 8, boxes 4B and B1-2).

Discussion 
The analysis and modelling presented here serves to formalise concepts that often remain 
underspecified in the narrative literature on PTSD and related psychopathology. In this work, 
context refers to elements of the sensory or cognitive periphery that provide evidence for the 
states of affairs prior to belief-updating. Belief-updating here refers to inferring ‚Äì and learning 
about ‚Äì the causes of sensations through neuronal message passing and experience -
dependent plasticity, respectively. Our simulations demonstrate the interdependence between

inference and learning, and how biased inference can lead to aberrant learning and a failure 
to recognise changes in context. It is this failure we associate with the psychopathology of 
PTSD. Biased inference in this instance manifests as a failure of responding to epistemic 
affordances that leads to a failure to recognise the context has changed. 
 
This kind of failure sits on a critical tipping point, leading to a phenomenal description of the 
ensuing inferences in terms of bistability, that can be related to the notion of canalisation in 
psychopathology.40 While attractor basins are used in different ways to indicate neuronal 
dynamics, here, we have shown that plasticity can create unstable basins that bias the system 
toward sensory evidence accumulation to resolve ambiguity about the causes of sensations. 
In contrast, a collapse or canalisation into a single basin can cause a trajectory initiated by a 
sensory cue to become inescapably bound by the basin; resolving uncertainty through precise 
‚Äì but false ‚Äì beliefs about the causes of sensations. Crucially, th is resolution is self -
maintaining because it precludes epistemic foraging or information seeking; thereby 
instantiating a vicious circle of confirmation bias. Restoring plasticity can make the system 
amenable to new learning, thus rebalancing priors and re storing the collapsed basin. The 
saddle point between basins corresponds to a reinstatement of context-sensitivity.  
 
In a context where harm is highly likely, such as a combat zone, it is adaptive to have elevated 
ACh and NA, to remain vigilant against possible harm , and rapidly mobilize a defensive 
response for mitigating harm. Essentially, it is context -sensitive to be in a heightened alert 
state, ‚Äòbetter safe than sorry‚Äô. After a transition into a context where harm is unlikely, it does 
not mean harm is impossible. A home neighbourhood may be largely harmless, but 
occasionally harms can arise. Thus, context-sensitivity requires sensory disambiguation on a 
case-by-case bas is, as  a tap on the shoulder could be from a friend or foe. It would be 
maladaptive to immediately respond to a shoulder tap with a vigorous fight/flight response, as 
this would be unwarranted if it were a friend. Yet, it would also be maladaptive to be ‚Äòfearless‚Äô 
and disregard learning whether the tap is indeed from a foe, which warrants self-protection.  
 
We have provided modelling-informed analysis of PTSD pathogenesis and related sequelae 
of traumatic stress and their relationship to diagnostic criteria in terms of vulnerability and 
resilience. Our key hypothesis is that the balance of plasticity -enhancement and plasticity -
decay for aversive events is greater in the vulnerable. We have also provided several novel 
auxiliary hypotheses that can be tested in controlled experiments  (see above and Online 
Methods). 
 
Our analysis also provides a way to understand learning -based treatment mechanisms and 
how they might relate to (e.g.) monoaminergic interventions and substance abuse.  
Specifically, pharmacological interventions may enhance psychotherapies that support 
learning contextual disambiguation, through allocating attention to evidence for benign causes 
of current events, including aversive ones. This is a putative mechanism of action of 
entactogen-assisted psychotherapy. The combination of psychotherapy and psycho -
pharmacology may be indispensable in some cases (e.g., due to TBI -related structural 
damage). At the same time, our account is consistent with the position that in other cases, 
psychotherapy could provide sufficient PTSD treatment on its own. 
 
For onset prevention, the model suggests that arbitrary aspects of the environment during the 
post-traumatic period could hinder or induce impairment, depending on how strongly and/or 
frequently sensory cues mobilise a defensive response. For example, imme diate debriefing 
following a traumatic experience could unduly remobilise a defensive response and strengthen 
memory of the adverse context. Social factors (e.g., support groups, transition homes) would 
have the potential to counterbalance adverse cued responses by strengthening the inference 
of a present harmless context. Overall, our analysis lends support to biopsychosocial 
approaches to diagnosing and treating PTSD, or preventing its onset.

Recognition dynamics involving foreground and background elements 
Consider the example of being in a room with red wallpaper and a table with a deck of playing 
cards on it. This example can be linked to disparate literature using several terminological 
variations; namely, the room and wallpaper properties can be understood variously as context, 
background, scene, sensory periphery and/or global visual field. When interacting with the 
deck of cards, the deck can be considered a foreground or salient element, an object of 
attentional or sensory focus and/or part of the loca l visual field. In what follows, we treat the 
terms within each set as synonymous, acknowledging that some research operationalises 
distinctions internal to each set. 
 
In an ordinary setting, relative to an agent‚Äôs attentional focus and interaction, there are often 
focal elements that are salient (i.e., foreground) and surrounding scene components that can 
be categorized as ‚Äòcontext‚Äô (i.e., background). In any such event , there also may be 
experiential and affective relationships between the agent and salient elements (e.g., the cards 
were a gift from a friend). How agent -environment interactions during an event unfold in 
perception will impinge on how they are encoded in  memory, including salient foreground, 
contextual background, affective, and goal-direct aspects (e.g., implicit or explicit reward). In 
turn, how they are recalled from memory will also differ according to these encoded aspects 
and the recall circumstances. 
 
Experimental studies often manipulate foreground and background elements as stimulus and 
context, to investigate neuropsychological processes ‚Äì for example, by displaying sequences 
of images to isolate a phenomenon of interest. One finding is that recognition of a paired object 
and scene mutually reinforce each other.41 A pairing can be learned by repeated exposure to 
examples (‚Äòtraining‚Äô), such as a red room with a deck of cards and a blue room with a large 
matchbox. After training, seeing a deck of cards will lead to fast recognition of the red room 
(inference from obj ect to context), and vice versa, from room colour to table contents 
(inference from context to object). 
 
It is also the case that scenes can be recognised based on global texture. For example, the 
red room may be full of round tables and chairs, while the blue room has rows of theatre seats. 
These scenes not only have dimensional features (red vs. blue) with recognisable objects 
(table seating vs. rows), but also a global textural stimulus array that combines into a gestalt 
and facilitates rapid recognition.42 Global context also can be multimodal, such that different 
ambient sounds in each room (auditory back ground), once learned, facilitate faster visual 
recognition.43,44 
 
All of the above forms of recognition (stimulus -context associations) can be recast as 
disambiguation in probabilistic inference. On -going predictions govern visual scanning 
(sensory sampling) to accumulate evidence that lowers the uncertainty about a give n 
hypothesis. This describes the unimpaired ‚Äòmachinery‚Äô that gives rise to PTSD when deployed 
suboptimally. 
 
Continuing with the unimpaired scenario, when there is an element in common across multiple 
contexts, such as a curtain hanging in both the red and blue rooms, visually focussing on the 
curtain provides an ambiguous indication of what room I am in. If I hypothesise that I am in 
the blue room, I predict I will see rows of seats when I scan the room. Seeing round tables

Between ‚Äòbottom-up‚Äô and ‚Äòtop-down‚Äô: Visual working memory (VWM) 
In a cluttered scene ‚Äì akin to a naturalistic environment ‚Äì individual object recognition may be 
diminished for most objects, especially when scanning a scene with unfocused attention. 
However, preferred (i.e., familiar or unsurprising) objects may remain undiminished despite 
clutter,50 which can explain a ‚Äòpop -out‚Äô effect when scanning a crowd and spotting a familiar 
face. This preferential recognition occurs in the ventral stream, which has been found to be 
impaired in PTSD, resulting in a form of sensory  attenuation.51 A puzzle about sensory 
attenuation in PTSD is how it might relate to hypervigilance, a form of sensory hyperactivity. 
This puzzle can be solved from the account we elucidate here.  
 
Psychology and neuroimaging studies operationalise various load concepts (attentional, 
perceptual, cognitive, working memory) to focus on specific differences (e.g., visual pathways 
vs. attention networks). Yet the common ground among them is a resource model that can be 
mapped to neuronal populations with biophysical limits. For example, all neurons within a 
population can be tuned to a single stimulus, or a subset is tuned to (e.g.) another stimulus. 
 
From a technical perspective, this resource allocation is an emergent property of active 
inference in the following sense: the objective of active inference is to maximise the evidence 
for generative models of the lived world. Evidence (a.k.a., marginal li kelihood) can be 
decomposed into accuracy and complexity. Therefore, model selection necessarily entails a 
minimisation of complexity, which can be read as minimising the degrees of freedom used to 
provide an accurate account of the sensorium. 52‚Äì54 This mi nimisation can be read as 
complying with Occam‚Äôs principle and manifests as an apparent constraint on the allocation 
of representational resources (e.g., the quantisation of representations, implicit in receptive 
fields). Our current modelling does not aim  to disentangle different aspects of resource 
allocation within active inference. For our purposes, it suffices to say that mitigating harm in a 
traumatic encounter commonly requires visuospatial attention and sensorimotor coordination; 
namely, efficient active vision.55‚Äì58 
 
Visual working memory (VWM) is a useful construct for centring on load -limited interactions 
related to visuospatial sensorimotor task demands. We treat VWM as a limited neuronal 
population susceptible to ‚Äòbottom -up‚Äô (sensory driven) and ‚Äòtop -down‚Äô (recalle d imagery 
driven) excitation. 59‚Äì61 Given a sensory contribution, some findings on visual pathways 
unconcerned with VWM indirectly bear on our model. For instance, there is empirical support

A synthesis of the above evidence points to a possible explanation for ‚Äòflashback‚Äô 
phenomenology: high sensory precision or gain (modulated by ACh22, discussed in the main 
text) can facilitate visual attention to scene elements with enhanced salience (trauma 
reminders or ‚Äòcues‚Äô) that are rapidly identified and rapidly elicit a trauma -context association. 
The traumatic context is characterised as one with a high probability of ca uses of harm 
(‚Äòharmful context‚Äô).16 The on-going imperatives of harm -mitigation requires flexibly attending 
to ‚Äòtask-relevant‚Äô stimuli; i.e., sampling the visual scene to maximise information gain (a.k.a., 
Bayesian surprise).72‚Äì75 This aspect of active vis ion follows the metaphor of an attentional 
‚Äòspotlight‚Äô that must rapidly shift among momentary foreground elements. With each shift, the 
remaining background scene is relegated to a diminished ‚Äòtask -irrelevant‚Äô periphery.64 This 
restlessly roving spotlight would be indicative of hypervigilance, with increased sensory gain 
on a focal element and corresponding attenuation of the sensory periphery.

periphery is uninformative about context (from a traumatised first-person standpoint) because 
of high precision on the prior probability of the harmful context. In terms of belief -updating, 
given the (false) belief one is in a harmful context that matches traumatic recall, a strong top-
down influence of trauma scene imagery on VWM ‚Äì concurrent with foreground perception ‚Äì 
can cause a distortive blend of the present scene and a past traumatic event. 
 
These imagery-based elements would continuously support misperception and misrecognition 
of the current scene, allowing attention to be maintained on a significant foreground element 
while hindering attention to ‚Äì and subsequent belief updating about ‚Äì context (Fig. 8, box 4B). 
In turn, a behavioural response is elicited that would be appropriate to a repeat traumatic 
encounter, but is inappropriate to the veridical conditions. This formulation of sensory 
attenuation theory thus provides a testable hypothes is for the neurocognitive machinery of

sensing more than focal sensing (e.g., in a target/distractor paradigm). The theory also 
provides a testable hypothesis related to another common PTSD symptom, the exaggerated 
startle response (ESR); namely, that alternatively valenced contexts for the same probe 
stimulus should differentially affect startle parameters for PTSD -impaired individuals to a 
lesser degree than those without.76

Between past and present: From memory to generalisation 
An encounter with a hostile person or adverse place would be important to remember for harm 
mitigation in a subsequent encounter. On one hand, the widely accepted psychological 
account of this experiential process moves in a bottom-up fashion from perception to VWM to 
short-term and/or long-term memory (STM and LTM), and in top-down fashion from memory 
to recall and/or recognition. On the other hand, widely accepted hierarchical predictive 
processing accounts cast this overall process as involving a vast nu mber of feedback and 
feedforward re-entrant loops throughout the brain. This speaks to a strong correspondence 
between the psychological constructs and interacting yet discrete neuronal populations 
involved in active sensing and learning.29,61,77 Thus, our neurocomputational (active inference) 
model is compatible with both psychological and neurophysiological modes of explanation.47,78 
From this perspective, we draw on typically disconnected findings on PTSD including cognitive 
psychology and biological psy chiatry, and related work on unimpaired mechanisms and 
processes. 
 
In light of the above literature, it is clear that we can learn to associate foreground elements 
with background scenes, and foreground and background can independently activate one 
another. Such co-activation also applies to elements of episodic memories.  Events involving 
multiple observed elements (e.g., specific visual landmarks, faces, ambient sounds, etc.) are 
experienced and encoded together. If an element overlaps between events (a sunset viewed 
on two separate holiday destinations), a memory of both events may be partly activated when 
seeing another sunset. That is, similar neuronal representations may be partially coactivated 
(e.g., 50-50).  
 
An additional sensory stimulus associated with one memory but not the other (a specific scent) 
can in turn facilitate a (non-pathological) ‚Äòflashback‚Äô of the distinctive event memory.79,80 This 
could describe the famous Proustian phenomenological effect of having a madeleine with tea 
and being mentally transported to a vivid childhood scene as if it were present again. The 
‚Äòwinner takes all‚Äô convergence on a decision amounts to an inference : inhibiting ruled out 
representations and disinhibiting a selected one.81 
 
Cognitive psychological and neurobehavioural experiments are often designed to mimic a 
naturalistic progression that involves event -based experience, memory, generalisation, and 
recall. These can amount to learning that a task (e.g., pulling a lever) has a  desirable (i.e., 
familiar or preferred) result in one context and an undesirable (ambiguous or surprising) result 
in another. Learning about the task and the context can allow a contextual cue to elicit the 
learned context -relevant interactions (e.g., app roach vs. avoid). Human and nonhuman 
primate studies and computational simulations indicate that such task and context information 
is differentially encoded in memory.82‚Äì84 
 
Hippocampal dynamics are implicated in both generalised task guidance and episodic 
recall.29,80,85 This interaction between generalised task-oriented behaviour and context-specific 
episodic memories offers another example of unimpaired ‚Äòmachinery‚Äô involved in PTSD 
impairment. For example, if you move heavy boxes, you may learn a generalisable task -
relevant strategy for how to lift, hold, and carry. Months later, when moving furniture, an 
association between the two contexts can help guide your behaviour. At  the same time, the

event memories.85,87,88 Our modelling (see main text) shows how a harmful context can elicit

Impaired context disambiguation in post-traumatic pathology 
Non-pathological ‚Äòflashes‚Äô can have a similar genesis as pathological ‚Äòflashbacks‚Äô (a common 
PTSD symptom). Consider what may occur if a quotidian but non -ubiquitous object is part of 
a traumatic event, say, a readily available jacket worn by an aggressor in a hostile encounter. 
There is a potential for a later encounter with someone else wearing the same type of jacket 
to elicit a traumatic memory. 
 
Pursuing the above example, a resilient individual ‚Äì who experienced the traumatic event ‚Äì 
may form an initial trauma association with the jacket, which co-exists with other memories of 
seeing the same type of jacket on others. The association may be weakened over time, as the 
jacket cue is neuronally linked across distinct event boundaries. A repeat encounter with the 
cue is thus insufficient to disambiguate co -activated memories, so they do not become vivid 
or ‚Äòcapture‚Äô attention. Attention is easily depl oyed to the sensory periphery to accumulate 
evidence about the present external surroundings. It is unlikely the present situation will be 
confused with the past event. 
 
An illustration is given in Fig. 8, with a triangle standing in for the jacket. In Fig. 8 box A, the 
triangle is in common to both circles, depicting event memories as guiding contexts. Seeing 
the triangle evokes both events, with a square or cross context, respectively. Deploying 
attention to the sensory periphery (Fig. 8, box 4A) resolves the uncertainty about the present 
context. 
 
Using the same example, a vulnerable individual may find the jacket cue capable of fully 
activating the trauma recollection, at the expense of any competing associations (Fig. 8, filled-
in circle in box B1; the contrast to B2 is explained below). This full ‚Äòwinner takes all‚Äô activation 
can predominate in VWM, increasing recall vividness and associated affective and motor 
responses, intermingled with present perception (i.e., flashback; Fig. 8, box 4B). The present 
salient object (triangle/jacket) interacts with the recalled traumatic context, mutually reinforcing 
the belief that the past event is present, in other words, a re -experiencing. This suggests a 
further testable hypothesis about post -traumatic impairment that differentiates between an 
active inference account, which predicts increased weight on trauma -encoded context, and 
dual representation theory, which predicts decreased weight.91 
 
Under a sensory (periphery) attenuation policy, the vulnerable person does not learn about 
the veridical context. This reinforces a pathological persistent belief of being in the harmful 
context, given the cue (as illustrated in our model simulation  in the  main text ). If re -
experiencing is itself self-categorized as injurious, deliberate experiential avoidance will further 
impair learning. But even without deliberate experiential avoidance, the re -experiencing 
dynamics that limit peripheral awareness ultimately block inference and learning about benign 
causes of sensations (counterfactual blocking). 11 In contrast, if counterfactual evaluation of 
context is not blocked, it becomes possible to recognise the veridical context. Even minor 
indications that I am not in harmful context, if sustained (e.g., with adequate social support92), 
can facilitate a gradual updating and rebalancing of contextual priors that restores appropriate 
context-sensitivity. This rebalancing can be understood in terms of experience -dependent 
neuroplasticity. 
 
In naturalistic conditions, the restored context-sensitivity would be adaptive as opposed to an 
‚Äòextinguished fear‚Äô that could be maladaptive. For example, one may be momentarily 
susceptible to harm in an alleyway near home, even though the home context ha s a low 
likelihood of harm overall. A cue (jacket) that indicates a possible harmful encounter can be 
confirmed by rapid exploratory sensing (friend or foe) leading to an equally rapid decision 
selecting or deselecting defensive behaviour.

Traumatic memory and sleep 
Our analysis and modelling provides fresh insight into the finding that dysregulated sleep 
following traumatic experience predicts the development of PTSD. 95 As described above, 
stressful recall results in the maintenance of a wakeful trauma experience memory trace that 
is reactivated on recall but not re -encoded.93 There is another opportunity for re -encoding 
during sleep. Successful re -encoding facilitates a closed event boundary with selected 
associations neuronally linked across events (for instance, the jacket from the above example; 
see Fig. 8 box A and B1).88 However, if following a traumatic experience, subsequent sleep is 
disrupted, then the original traumatic memory trace may continue to be maintained (Fig. 8 box 
B2).24 
 
In a healthy scenario, memories are repeatedly re-activated (‚Äòretrieved‚Äô) and re-encoded in a 
new set of synaptic connections that are activated when recalling the original event. 96 The 
healthy progression depends upon unstressed retrieval and sufficient NREM sleep, both of 
which may face interference following acute or extended traumatic experience. 93,95,97 For 
example, early life stress can lead to a memory trace (neuronal ensemble) being preferentially 
re-activated during related adult stress,98 suggesting a contributing factor to CPTSD.  
 
In the initial days following an event, some elements of the event -correlated neuronal 
ensemble overlap between its initial encoding and subsequent re -encoding. In a healthy 
progression, the re -encoded ensemble eliminates overlap with the originally encode d 
ensemble. This process can be understood formally as information compression or model 
reduction to minimise complexity (i.e., maximise model evidence in the absence of sensory 
input),70 differentially driven by time and experiential learning factors. 99 These factors are 
captured indirectly (abstractly) by our modelling: the time factor can be understood as a decay 
rate which is potentially too low in a person vulnerable to post -traumatic syndromes. If there 
is insufficient decay over time, alternative syn aptic weights can be strengthened by learning 
(e.g., in treatment intervention). In principle, this learning could reduce excessive re-activation 
of the original ensemble, which may subsequently support its re-encoding. 
 
A single nightmare or even a period of nightmares (e.g., following a bereavement) is not 
necessarily pathological, but may contribute to a period of intrusive cued re -activations in a 
manner suggested by our model. While our framing and modelling deliberately excludes fear 
and extinction, related work analyses nightmares in terms of fear extinction linked to 
‚Äòdecontextualization‚Äô and ‚Äòrecontextualization‚Äô‚Äô.100 These terms can be re-interpreted using our 
model as follows. If there is a cue in common to multiple memories, an overly precise 
contextual prior for ‚Äòharmful‚Äô may preferentially activate a traumatic memory that gives rise to 
a nightmare. Weakening the p rior (synaptic strength) may reduce cued re -activation of that 
memory, akin to decontextualization of the cue. Strengthening a competing contextual prior 
for ‚Äòharmless‚Äô may then allow preferential activation of non -traumatic memories, akin to 
recontextualization of the cue.
